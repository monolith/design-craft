Colour theory — a visual reference

Side-by-side demonstrations of the colour science behind this skill: what is proven, what is convention, and what is myth. Many of the findings are debunks, which is why they read well as side-by-side contrasts.

How to read this: sections A–B are the evidence (harmony, preference, emotion, gradients, contrast, perception, the colour wheel); sections C–G apply it — the role-based palette system, art & architectural palettes, the Corbusier house default, and the chart palettes. Claims are tagged STRONG (proven), CONVENTION (established practice), or MYTH.

Recommended default — Corbusier (light · dark · watercolour) The house style, for both UI and charts: a restrained earth-tone family in three grounds. It is the default because it stacks what the evidence below endorses — a low-chroma ground, one reserved emphasis accent, lightness before hue — and it pairs with Tufte's data-ink. (For chart series a muted palette can't keep apart, switch the chart only to Tableau — §F.) The three theme cards are right below; full sample pages in §E, the 16-form chart gallery in §G; the evidence that justifies all of it is §A–B.

A · What survived verification Harmony and colour-emotion — the two areas with the strongest controlled-study support. Mostly debunks of popular beliefs, which is why they read as contrasts.

1 · "Harmony rules" don't predict a good pair — lightness contrast does CONVENTIONSTRONG

The complementary/analogous/triadic hue-wheel "schemes" have no validated power to predict preference (the foundational algorithm itself admits there's no empirical definition of harmony). A textbook-complementary pair can be unreadable; what actually predicts preference and legibility is liked component colours + lightness contrast.

Source: Cohen-Or et al. 2006 (no empirical harmony definition); Schloss & Palmer 2011 — harmony↔preference r=.79; full model with lightness-difference explains 80.8% of pair-preference variance.

2 · People reliably like some hues and dislike others (Ecological Valence) STRONG

Single-colour preference isn't innate or geometric — it tracks the average valence of the objects associated with a colour. A no-free-parameter model (WAVE) predicts it at r=0.893. Preference peaks at blue/cyan (clear sky, clean water) and troughs at chartreuse / dark-yellow / brown (rot, decay). Bar height = average preference.

Source: Palmer & Schloss 2010, PNAS 107:8877 — WAVE r=+0.893, ~80% of variance, no fitted parameters.

3 · Colour→emotion is real and dimensional — but many-to-many STRONGMYTH: one→one

Associations are systematic and broadly cross-cultural (132 studies, 42k people, 64 countries; cross-nation similarity r=.88), organised on stable dimensions — warm/cool, heavy/light, active/passive (predictable from colour coordinates at r≈.78–.81). They're driven by lightness/saturation/temperature, not the hue label. Swatches placed on two of those axes:

Why "blue = trust" is a myth: the mapping is many-to-many. Red also reads as love, danger, excitement, and anger by context; "calm" maps to blue, green, and pale pastels. The dimensions are real; the one-colour-one-emotion shortcut is not.

Source: Ou et al. 2018 (CIE TC1-86, 12 regions) — warm/cool .78, heavy/light .80, active/passive .81; Jonauskaite et al. 2020 (30 nations); 132-study systematic review (1895–2022).

4 · "Romantic red" (red boosts attractiveness) — the cautionary myth MYTH / weak-null

The textbook example of overstated colour psychology. The meta-analytic effect is small, shrinks when an outlier is removed, and reverses to ~zero in pre-registered studies — with significant publication bias. Each row is a point estimate + 95% CI against the no-effect line (a forest plot — the charting-skill form).

Source: Lehmann, Elliot & Calin-Jageman 2018 (meta-analysis, 41 studies / 6,682 participants) — men→women d=0.26→0.19; women→men d=0.13; pre-registered d=−0.10; publication-bias r=.52.

B · More — established colour science These rest on canonical, well-cited colour and perception science — established results (gradients, contrast, simultaneous contrast, Mach bands, the colour wheel) that the palette system downstream leans on.

5 · A perceptually-smooth gradient vs naive RGB STRONG

Interpolating two colours straight in RGB drags the midpoint toward a muddy grey "dead zone"; a perceptually-uniform map (viridis) keeps lightness rising smoothly with no false bands.

✗ blue→yellow interpolated in RGB — note the grey dead-zone mid-way
✓ viridis — perceptually uniform, monotonic lightness

Source: Kovesi 2015 (uniform-lightness colormaps); viridis — Smith & van der Walt 2015. Established colour science.

6 · Vibrating boundaries — saturated complementaries at equal lightness PERCEPTION

Pure saturated red on pure blue (similar luminance, opposite hue) does two distinct things. The edge shimmers — an equiluminant-border instability: the luminance channel sees almost no contrast across the edge, so the form system can't lock it down. And the red seems to float forward — that's chromostereopsis (long-wavelength red focuses slightly nearer than short-wavelength blue). The fix for both is the same: give the pair luminance contrast, not just a hue difference.

✓ LIGHTNESS CONTRAST

Source: the float/depth is chromostereopsis (Einthoven 1885; Thompson, May & Stone 1993); the edge shimmer is equiluminant-border instability (the luminance system can't localise an iso-luminant edge). Established perception.

7 · Simultaneous contrast — the same grey, two surrounds STRONG

Both centre squares are the identical grey (#8a8a8a). On the dark surround it looks lighter; on the light surround, darker. A colour's appearance depends on what's around it — always judge colours in context, never in isolation.

Source: simultaneous contrast (Chevreul 1839; Albers, Interaction of Color). Established colour science.

8 · Functional palette roles + alert tiers CONVENTION + WCAG

Assign colour to roles, not vibes: a dominating quiet background, a high-contrast foreground, one reserved highlight, and a critical channel with multiple tiers (info → warning → severe) that stay distinguishable and not-colour-alone (icon + word, differing in lightness so they survive colourblindness).

9 · Mach bands — the eye sharpens every edge (lean on it) STRONG

At each step in this grey ramp, a brighter sliver appears on the lighter side of the edge and a darker one on the darker side — bands that aren't in the pixels. The visual system enhances luminance edges; it's free edge-pop for stepped legends and banded encodings.

Source: Mach bands (Mach 1865; Ratliff 1965 — lateral inhibition). Established perception.

10 · The colour wheel — a real circle, unproven rules STRONG: the circleCONVENTION: the harmony spokes

The wheel isn't arbitrary: hue is carried by two opponent channels — red–green and blue–yellow (Hering 1878; modelled psychophysically by Hurvich & Jameson 1957, then confirmed in opponent neurons by De Valois et al. 1958) — so arranging hue in a ring is genuine perceptual structure. But it's usually drawn in two misleading ways. (1) Equal angles ≠ equal perceptual steps. The familiar HSL / RGB wheel (left) spaces hue by maths, so its yellows and cyans read pale and its blues read dark — the ring is lumpy (its WCAG lightness swings from ≈0.07 at blue to ≈0.93 at yellow). A perceptually-uniform wheel (right — OKLCh, Ottosson 2020, the model modern tools use) holds lightness even all the way round. Choose categorical colours on the uniform ring, not the lumpy one. (2) The "harmony" spokes — complementary (opposite), triadic (120° apart) — are convention, not a validated recipe for a good pairing (§1: what actually predicts a liked pair is lightness contrast + how much each colour is liked). Use the wheel to space hues apart and to navigate — not to certify beauty.

(3) How to actually use the wheel — the standard combinations. Pick a base hue, then read the conventional relationships straight off the ring: complementary (the hue opposite, 180°) for maximum contrast; monochromatic (one hue varied by tint [+ white], tone [+ grey] and shade [+ black]) — the most versatile, and the scheme that leans hardest on lightness contrast rather than hue; analogous (neighbours, ±30°) for a calm, low-contrast set; triadic (evenly-spaced thirds, 120°) for a balanced multi-category set; split-complementary (the two hues flanking the complement) for contrast with less tension; tetradic (two complementary pairs forming a square) for four categories. The spokes below show each as a shape on the wheel — the same combinations interactive pickers (Canva, Adobe Color) expose. The honest caveat (§1): these are convention — a shared vocabulary and a reliable way to space categorical hues apart, not a guarantee of a pleasing result. Read the scheme off the wheel to get candidates, then pick the actual colours by lightness contrast + CVD-safety + liked-hue, and verify.

Source: opponent-process colour vision (Hering 1878; Hurvich & Jameson, Psychological Review 1957; physiological confirmation De Valois et al. 1958); OKLab / OKLCh perceptual space (Ottosson 2020); harmony-as-convention per §1 (Schloss & Palmer 2011). The hue circle is perceptual; the spokes off it are taste.

C · A refined palette system — modern, light, airy, timeless Perception rules + Tufte restraint + the verified colour research + modern design, in one light-leaning family. Colour is spent on two jobs only: one bold emphasis for text, and an escalating state channel.

11 · One palette, five themes — White · Off-white · Linen · Dark · Paper

One role system across a true White, a neutral Off-white, a warm Linen, a clean Dark, and a soft-gray Paper — each on a strict dominance budget (the proportion bar on every card): Two dominant tones — a single ground and the ink — fill the screen (off-white not pure white, near-black not pure black; perception §6: drop the luminance, keep the contrast). There is no separate "surface" colour: panels are split by a hairline, not a second fill — Tufte's smallest effective difference, so no ink is spent where a line will do. Up to two supporting colours, used sparingly — a bold emphasis for important text and a quiet info that blends; and alerts in reserve — warning gentle but alerting, severe set clearly apart (most saturated, thickest rule), each with icon + word, never colour alone. Components stay in-palette: header / nav from ground + ink, links from the emphasis accent. (White keeps black ink but draws its accents from the art/photography register.) Live contrast under every card.

Source: semantic colour-role tokens (Material 3, Radix Colors, IBM Carbon); WCAG 1.4.3 / 1.4.11 contrast; 1.4.1 not-colour-alone. Structure = established best practice; the per-theme contrast is verified live on screen.

15 · Tableau charts — light & dark (the categorical fallback)

When the muted set can't separate enough series, switch the chart (only the chart) to a proven categorical palette — Tableau 10 / 20 (Maureen Stone), engineered for categorical separation and reasonable colourblind spacing. The light variant is the standard Tableau 20; the dark variant is brightened so each hue clears the dark ground. Two rules still bind: cap rapidly-identified categories at ~7 (Healey) — the rest of the 20 is for legend / reference / one-colour-per-panel — and direct-label the series so colour is never the only cue (the labels survive colourblindness). The swatch strip under each chart is the full 20.

Source: Tableau 10 / 20 categorical palettes (Maureen Stone, Tableau); ≤7 rapid-ID cap (Healey 1996); direct labelling not colour-only (WCAG 1.4.1 / §2). For data, not chrome — and only when Corbusier's restraint isn't enough.
