Perception & colour — see the difference

Nine worked demos for the graphical-perception-and-color skill. Each shows one evidence-backed rule by contrast — wrong beside right, or channel beside channel — and cites its primary study. Zero dependencies; renders over plain HTTP.

1 · Encoding hierarchy — the same values, four channels

Read the order off each panel. Position gives it instantly; length still works; area and colour are guesses. Accuracy of decoding runs position → length → angle → area → colour.

Evidence: Cleveland & McGill (1984), JASA 79(387) — graphical-perception ranking; replicated by Heer & Bostock (2010), CHI.

2 · Categorical colour — how many hues, and the legend trap

Around 5–7 colours are reliably told apart at a glance; past that, identification degrades. A hue-only legend forces the eye to ferry to the key — label the series in place.

Evidence: Healey (1996), IEEE Vis — ~5–7 rapidly-identifiable colours; WCAG 2.x SC 1.4.1 Use of Color; ColorBrewer — Harrower & Brewer (2003).

3 · Sequential — monotonic lightness vs rainbow

A continuous ramp must rise monotonically in lightness. Viridis does; rainbow/jet doesn't — its luminance zig-zags, inventing false boundaries and hiding real detail.

Evidence: Borland & Taylor (2007), IEEE CG&A — rainbow colormap harmful; Kovesi (2015), arXiv:1509.03700 — uniform-lightness design.

4 · Diverging vs sequential, on signed data

Data with a meaningful midpoint (above/below zero) needs a diverging scale — two hues, light at the break. A sequential ramp hides the sign and the zero crossing.

Evidence: ColorBrewer typology — Harrower & Brewer (2003), Cartographic Journal.

5 · Colour-vision deficiency — simulate, and encode lightness

~8% of men have red-green CVD. The red-green pair collapses under a deuteranopia simulation; a palette that differs in lightness (not just hue) survives it.

Evidence: prevalence — Nuñez, Anderton & Renslow (2018), PLOS ONE; simulation matrix — Machado, Oliveira & Fernandes (2009), IEEE TVCG.

Palettes — ten that cover the real cases, and when to use each

Match the palette type to the data first (nominal → categorical; ordered → sequential; signed → diverging; not-data → neutral+accent), then pick within it. Prefer colourblind-safe defaults (Okabe–Ito, viridis) unless you have a reason not to.

Evidence: Okabe & Ito (2008) — CUD safe set; viridis — Smith & van der Walt (2015); Cividis — Nuñez et al. (2018); ColorBrewer — Harrower & Brewer (2003).

6 · Contrast — the only binding standard (WCAG), computed live

Each ratio below is computed from the actual colours: text needs ≥4.5:1, data marks and lines ≥3:1, measured against the real background. The faint ones fail.

Evidence: WCAG 2.x — SC 1.4.3 (text contrast), SC 1.4.11 (non-text / graphical objects). Ratio = (L1+0.05)/(L2+0.05) on relative luminance.

7 · Background — brightness, not hue (incl. dark done right)

The same chart on four grounds. Comfort tracks brightness + contrast, not hue — the "calming" green isn't restful, just dimmer. Light beats dark for small dense text, but for glanceable charts neither wins — offer both. Whatever the ground, re-check contrast.

Evidence: Buchner & Baumgartner (2007); Piepenbrock et al. (2013/2014), Ergonomics — polarity for small text; While & Sarvghad (2024) — no clear winner for charts.

Dashboards & lists — consistency, one accent, CVD-safe status

Most people build these. Keep a consistent palette across tiles, reserve a single accent for the one thing that matters, and never encode status by hue alone — red-amber-green dots fail for ~8% of men. Back the colour with a shape or a word. (The dashboard and list forms themselves live in the charting skill; this is their colour.)

Evidence: WCAG 2.x SC 1.4.1 — don't rely on colour alone; Okabe & Ito (2008); CVD prevalence — Nuñez et al. (2018).
