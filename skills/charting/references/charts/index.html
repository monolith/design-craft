Each section names the chart form and, in italics beneath it, the single Tufte technique it is built to demonstrate. The charts share one quiet palette and one serif. They render offline; the only links on this page are the stylesheet and the module at the bottom.

⚠ Anti-patterns — what not to do (and the fix)

The ✗ charts below are DELIBERATELY WRONG — shown only so they can be recognized and avoided. Never reproduce a ✗ pattern. Each sits beside its ✓ correction of the same data; when you build a chart, always use the ✓ version.

1 · Truncated y-axis vs. zero baseline

✗ Don't

Why this misleads: a non-zero baseline inflates the perceived gap between near-equal bars (Correll 2020; Yang 2021).

✓ Do

The fix: start the bar axis at zero — the bars read as the ~equal values they actually are.

2 · Bubble sized by radius vs. by area

✗ Don't

Why this misleads: radius ∝ value makes area grow with value², so a 4× value looks ~16× bigger (Pandey 2015; Tufte's Lie Factor).

✓ Do

The fix: radius ∝ √value so area is proportional to value (better still, use a position axis).

3 · Dual y-axis vs. two small panels

✗ Don't

Why this misleads: two independently-scaled y-axes can be slid until unrelated series "track" — a manufactured correlation.

✓ Do

The fix: give each series its own small panel (or index both to 100); no shared frame to fake a relationship.

4 · Pie chart vs. sorted bars

✗ Don't

Why this misleads: a pie encodes value as angle and slice area — both low-accuracy channels (Cleveland & McGill 1984). Near-equal slices can't be ranked, and the legend forces the eye to ferry back and forth.

✓ Do

The fix: sorted bars (or a dot plot) on one common length scale — the ranking reads instantly and each value is labeled in place.

5 · Stacked bars vs. grouped (common baseline)

✗ Don't

Why this misleads: only the bottom series sits on a common baseline; the middle series (accent) floats on a shifting base, so it's decoded as an unaligned length — try to read product B's trend.

✓ Do

The fix: grouped bars (or small multiples) put every series on a shared zero baseline, so B's quarter-to-quarter pattern reads directly.
