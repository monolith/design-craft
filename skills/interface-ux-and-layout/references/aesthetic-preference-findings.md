## The core answer: PROCESSING FLUENCY [STRONG]
People like an interface without knowing why mainly because of **processing fluency** — the easier the brain processes a screen (clear, high-contrast, conventional/prototypical, visually simple), the more positive the feeling, which gets **misread as "I like this."**
- A single fluency mechanism **unifies** preference for familiarity, symmetry, prototypicality, and figure-ground contrast — each eases processing → triggers positive affect (Reber, Schwarz & Winkielman 2004, *PSPR*).
- **It's a genuine feeling, not just a judgment:** easy-to-process stimuli measurably raise zygomaticus ("smile muscle") EMG (Winkielman & Cacioppo 2001).
- **It's a misattribution below awareness:** an unnoticed 25ms prime shifts beauty ratings; the effect **disappears when the good feeling is attributed to an external source** (e.g., background music) — direct proof the feeling *causes* the liking.
- **#1 actionable UI lever:** raise fluency and you buy liking "for free" — high figure-ground **contrast**, clarity, convention, restraint, alignment, good Gestalt.

## Prototypicality & MAYA [STRONG]
- **Prototypical/typical designs are preferred *because* they're easier to process** — prototypicality predicts both faster categorization and rated attractiveness, and fluency statistically **mediates** the link. (= the psychological basis for "follow convention.")
- **MAYA** ("most advanced yet acceptable"): typicality and novelty are **jointly and equally** predictive of preference and suppress each other — neither correlates alone. [STRONG for *products*; not tested on web specifically.]

## First impressions & visual complexity [STRONG, two sub-claims 2-1]
- Appeal forms **pre-consciously in 17–50 ms** and is **highly stable** (50ms ↔ 500ms r≈.95). (Lindgaard 2006)
- **Best first impression = LOW visual complexity + HIGH prototypicality**; worst = high-complexity + low-prototypicality (Tuch 2012, Reinecke 2013).
- **Visual complexity drives appeal more than colorfulness** [2-1 — "best current evidence," not settled].
- **For websites, simpler is monotonically better — Berlyne's inverted-U does NOT apply to web UI** [2-1; the broader complexity literature is genuinely mixed].

## Caveats
- **External validity:** the core fluency/contrast experiments used abstract stimuli (gray circles, line drawings); MAYA used physical products (cars/phones). Strong priors for UI but not tested *on* interfaces there; the complexity/first-impression work (Tuch/Reinecke/Lindgaard) IS on real websites.
- **Demographic moderation:** the "low complexity, restrained color" optimum is a *population average* (moderated by gender/age/education/country; Reinecke & Gajos 2014).
- **Mechanism debate:** "fluency carries inherent positive affect" vs "amplifies existing affect" (Albrecht & Carbon 2014; Graf & Landwehr 2015) — predictions converge for the positive/neutral stimuli UI lives in, so the practical conclusion holds.
