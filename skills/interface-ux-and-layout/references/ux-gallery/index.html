Part 1 · Foundations — the usability & human-factors evidence

The evidence first — what's proven, what's convention

Interface design has a real research base: human factors, experimental psychology, and accessibility standards. This gallery leads with that evidence — the layout and component examples that follow are applications of it, and the whole pages at the end put them together. Where controlled research runs out, the fallback is established best practice from a named authority (NN/g, Material, Apple HIG, GOV.UK), tagged honestly. Labels: STRONG controlled study / normative standard · CONVENTION established practice, weak/observational · MYTH the evidence contradicts the popular claim.

APerception & attention

  • People scan, don't read; the F-pattern is a failure state of unformatted text, not a layout to design for. STRONG-desc
  • Front-load the first ~2 words (~11 chars) of headings & links — the rest is skipped.
  • Legibility is driven by luminance / figure-ground contrast, not hue.
NN/g eye-tracking · WCAG · graphical-perception-and-color

BMotor control — Fitts's Law

  • Movement time falls as targets get bigger and closer; screen edges/corners are "infinite" targets. STRONG
  • Empirical touch minimum ~9–10 mm; WCAG 2.2 = 24×24 px (or spacing). 44 pt / 48 dp are comfortable defaults, not the floor.
Fitts 1954 · Parhi 2006 · WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8

CChoice & memory

  • Hick's Law RT = a + b·log₂(n) is settled — but the slope isn't invariant, so "fewer items is always faster" is a MYTH.
  • Response time: ~0.1 s feels instant; ~10 s is the attention limit (Miller — "best guesses"). CONVENTION
  • Recognition over recall — show the options, don't make people remember them.
Hick 1952 · Proctor & Schneider 2018 · Miller 1968 · Nielsen

DNavigation & IA

  • Visible / combo nav beats hidden (hamburger on desktop): −20% discoverability, ≥39% slower. STRONG
  • Broad & shallow beats narrow & deep — depth raises reaction time, errors, perceived complexity.
NN/g hidden-nav test · Jacko & Salvendy 1996

EFirst impressions & fluency

  • Appeal forms in 17–50 ms and barely changes after. STRONG
  • Processing fluency (clarity, contrast, convention, restraint) → liking; the brain misreads ease as "I like this."
  • For the web, simpler is monotonically better; complexity hurts more than colourfulness.
Lindgaard 2006 · Reber 2004 · Tuch 2012 · Reinecke 2013

FTesting & accessibility

  • Heuristic eval needs 3–5 evaluators (one finds 20–51%); "5 users = 85%" is a floor with huge variance. STRONG
  • WCAG 2.x contrast: 4.5:1 text, 3:1 marks — from luminance, not hue; never colour alone (1.4.1). normative
Nielsen & Molich 1990 · Faulkner 2003 · W3C WCAG 2.2

Everything below applies this. Part 2 is the evidence as layout, Part 3 as components, Part 4 in practice, and Part 5 assembles whole pages.

13 · Error prevention & recovery

Catch problems early, in place, in plain language — and say how to fix them. A vague after-the-fact error is the worst of both.

14 · Fitts's law · forgiveness over interrogation

Frequent/important actions should be bigger and closer (and edges are "infinite" targets). And prefer an undo to a confirm dialog — it's faster and less punishing.

I · Newly-verified practical evidence (2026 research)

25 · Don't hide primary navigation behind a ☰

Collapsing desktop nav into a hamburger measurably hurts. In NN/g's controlled test (179 users, 6 sites) hidden nav cut content discoverability >20%, made desktop tasks ≥39% slower, and was used far less than visible/combo (desktop 27% vs 48–50%; mobile 57% vs 86%). Keep primary nav visible; reserve fully-hidden nav for genuinely space-constrained mobile.

✗ HIDDEN · HAMBURGER (desktop)
Acme☰

Everything behind one icon — −20% discoverability, ≥39% slower, used by ~27%.

✓ VISIBLE · COMBO
AcmeProductsPricingDocsAbout☰

Primary items visible; ☰ kept only for overflow.

Evidence: NN/g hidden-navigation test — 179 participants, 6 sites, controlled. [STRONG]

26 · Broad & shallow beats narrow & deep

Jacko & Salvendy (1996) crossed menu breadth × depth in a controlled study: reaction time, error rate, and perceived complexity all rose as hierarchies got deeper. Fewer levels means fewer clicks to anything and less to hold in mind — go wide before you go deep.

27 · Touch targets — 24px is the floor, ~1 cm is the evidence

The measured minimum for one-handed thumb use is ~9–10 mm (Parhi 2006); error rate stops improving above ~9.6 mm. WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8 sets a 24×24 CSS-px minimum — or an undersized target passes via the spacing exception (a 24px circle on each target must not overlap another). Apple's 44 pt and Material's 48 dp are comfortable defaults, not the floor.

If a target must be smaller than 24px, keep ≥24px of clear spacing around it.

Evidence: Parhi 2006 (MobileHCI), controlled [STRONG]; WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8 [normative].

28 · "Five users finds 85%" — a floor, not a guarantee

For expert review, one evaluator finds only 20–51% of problems — run 3–5 (Nielsen & Molich). For user testing, "5 users = 85%" is a mean with huge variance: random 5-user sets caught 55–99% (Faulkner 2003), and on a large open-task e-commerce site the first five caught only ~35% (Spool & Schroeder). Five is a floor for cheap iteration — test more on big, complex, open-ended sites.

J · Why a web UI feels right — first impressions & fluency

29 · Front-load the first two words

A scanning eye fixates the first ~2 words (~11 characters) of a heading or link and skips the rest — so generic "click here / read more" is nearly invisible to a scanner (and to a screen-reader link list). Put the information-bearing word first.

✗ GENERIC · BACK-LOADED
  • Click here to see our pricing
  • Read more about the API
  • Learn more here →
✓ FRONT-LOADED
  • Pricing & plans
  • API reference
  • Security overview

Evidence: NN/g eye-tracking — fixations concentrate on the first words. [STRONG-descriptive · CONVENTION prescriptive]

30 · Fluency — the easier it reads, the more it's liked

A first impression forms in 17–50 ms and barely changes with more time (Lindgaard 2006). The lever is processing fluency: high figure-ground contrast, clarity, convention and restraint make a screen easy to process, and the brain misreads that ease as "I like this" (Reber 2004).
