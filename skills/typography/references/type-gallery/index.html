Part 1 · Legibility — proven vs myth

1 · Serif vs sans — no inherent reading advantage STRONGMYTH: "serif reads better"

Under matched conditions, fonts that differ only in serifs show no legibility or reading-speed difference (Arditi & Cho 2005; Times and Helvetica are "interchangeable", Daxer 2022). The same paragraph below reads equally well either way — so choose serif or sans for voice, not for "readability".

SERIF — Source Serif

SANS — Inter

A standard is a promise. The number on the tag stayed put; the tape measure did not. No single decision broke it — the absence of anyone defending it did.

Source: Arditi & Cho 2005 (Vision Research); Daxer 2022. Pick by tone; set it legibly.

2 · Preference ≠ reading speed STRONG

Within one person, reading speed varies ~35% across fonts with no comprehension loss, and which font someone prefers correlates near-zero with how fast they read it (r≈−0.13; Wallace et al., TOCHI 2022). "This one looks fresher / feels nicer" is a real signal about taste — not a claim about performance. Label it as such.

"Looks better" and "reads faster" are different measurements. This skill keeps them separate: legibility levers (size, x-height, spacing, contrast, hierarchy) are proven; the choice of voice is taste, chosen on top of a legible setting.

Source: Wallace et al. 2022 (ACM TOCHI); Rello & Baeza-Yates 2016.

3 · ALL-CAPS and wide tracking don't help body text STRONG against

ALL-CAPS only helps at tiny / low-vision sizes (the letters are physically bigger); at body size the advantage vanishes (Arditi & Cho 2007). Wide letter-spacing speeds single-word recognition but gives no words-per-minute gain and can slow skilled readers (Korinth 2020). Both hurt a paragraph.

✗ ALL-CAPS + LOOSE TRACKING

✓ SENTENCE CASE, NORMAL TRACKING

Source: Arditi & Cho 2007; Korinth, Gerstenberger & Fiebach 2020. Caps/tracking are for short labels & display, not prose.

Part 2 · Size, measure & leading

4 · x-height, not point size, is the legibility metric STRONG

The x-height fraction ranges 0.28–0.58 of body size across faces (mean 0.46; Karow) — so two fonts at the same px can differ ~2× in actual letter height. Both lines below are 28px; the taller x-height reads bigger and clearer at a glance. Judge size by x-height, and prefer high-x-height faces for UI, body and data.

Source: Karow 1993; Legge & Bigelow 2011. Critical print size ≈ 0.2° x-height (~9pt at 40cm); a broad "fluent range" above it where bigger buys comfort, not speed.

5 · Measure — keep lines ~45–75 characters long-line harmthe 66 target

Very long lines (~100 characters) measurably hurt comprehension vs ~55 (Dyson & Haselgrove 2001). The ~45–75 / "66" target is Bringhurst convention and the literature is mixed — so treat it as a sane band. The eye loses the start of the next line when the measure runs too wide.

✓ ~62 CHARACTERS — comfortable

6 · Leading — ~1.4–1.6× for prose CONVENTIONWCAG 1.4.12

Lines set too tight crowd vertically and the eye loses its place; comfortable leading (~1.4–1.6× the body size) lets each line breathe. WCAG 1.4.12 also requires text to survive a user setting line-height to 1.5× without breaking.

✓ line-height 1.55 — comfortable

Source: Material 3 (1.5× body heuristic); W3C WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.12. Tighter for big display, looser for small/long measure.

Part 3 · Scale & hierarchy

7 · Clear hierarchy aids scanning AND recall STRONG

Hierarchy isn't just looks. Eye-tracking shows the "layer-cake" scan (gaze rides headings) is the most effective scanning mode (NN/g), and controlled reading studies show headings raise recall and memory of topic structure (Lorch; Hyönä). A flat wall of text tends to push readers into the less-efficient F-pattern (NN/g).

8 · A type scale is for consistency, not because a ratio is "correct" CONVENTION

Modular scales (a constant ratio between steps) are a useful consistency tool but not evidence-based — major systems (Material 3, IBM Carbon) deliberately use non-constant ramps. Use a scale to keep sizes in a tidy set; build hierarchy first with size, weight, space and colour within one family.

10 · One family is often enough — emphasis within it first CONVENTION — Tim Brown

Before reaching for a second typeface, exhaust weight, size, space, case and colour in one good family. A single family (here IBM Plex Sans) carries a full hierarchy on its own — Brown's rule: pick the body face as the anchor first, since text faces survive every size.

Bold weight = primary heading
Semibold = subheading
Regular = body text, the anchor of the whole system.
Caps + muted = metadata
One emphasised phrase uses the reserved accent.

Source: Tim Brown, A Pocket Guide to Combining Typefaces. Two typefaces is usually plenty; rarely more than three.

Part 5 · Data typography (composes with charting)

13 · Tabular lining figures for any column of numbers CONVENTION+ mechanical fact

In a number column, tabular figures (every digit the same width, tabular-nums) align vertically so the eye scans magnitude instantly; proportional figures (varying widths) wobble and break the column. Old-style figures (with descenders) are for running prose, not tables. Same numbers below, three settings:

14 · Small-size legibility — open apertures, disambiguated digits, ≥14px charts STRONGONS 14px

Chart and table text is small and read at a glance, where letter recognition is the bottleneck (Legge). Open apertures measurably raise glance recognition (Beier & Oderkerk 2021), and a slashed zero / distinct 1·l·I prevents misreads. Keep chart text ≥14px (ONS; 12px only in small multiples) and direct-label over legends.

Part 6 · Cognitive load & the disfluency myth

15 · Effortless reading lowers load — don't degrade type to "help" STRONGMYTH: "ugly fonts aid memory"

Easy-to-process text (legible, conventional, well-spaced, high-contrast) lowers reading effort and even reads as more likeable (fluency). The famous claim that harder-to-read fonts improve memory came from a tiny study (n=28) and failed to replicate at scale — Sans Forgetica showed no benefit across ~300 people and a 1-week test. Don't make type harder to read; spend the proven levers.

✗ DISFLUENT — low contrast, small, tight

Each individual adjustment was defensible. A label nudged half a centimetre to flatter; a competitor matched; a decade passed. No single decision broke the standard.

✓ FLUENT — clear, conventional, spaced

Each individual adjustment was defensible. A label nudged half a centimetre to flatter; a competitor matched; a decade passed. No single decision broke the standard.

Source: disfluency replication failures — Huff & Maxwell 2022 (~300 participants); Taylor et al.; review Thiessen, Beier & Keage 2020. Genuine desirable difficulties (self-testing) work; disfluent type does not.

Part 7 · Accessibility of type

16 · Respect the user's spacing & size WCAG 2.2 AA

Build so users can override type without breakage. Set sizes in rem/em (not px), never disable zoom, and don't justify body text.

1.4.12 Text Spacing — survive line-height 1.5×, paragraph 2× font size, letter 0.12em, word 0.16em.
1.4.4 Resize Text — usable at 200% zoom.
1.4.10 Reflow — no 2-D scrolling down to 320 CSS px (≡ 1280px @ 400%).
1.4.8 (AAA) — measure ≤ 80 chars, leading ≥ 1.5×, not justified.

Source: W3C WCAG 2.2 Understanding docs (1.4.4 / 1.4.8 / 1.4.10 / 1.4.12). Contrast itself lives in graphical-perception-and-color.

17 · "Dyslexia-friendly fonts" don't help — plain sans + spacing does MYTH

Controlled studies find no reading benefit from OpenDyslexic/Dyslexie — often they read worse than Arial/Verdana (Wery & Diliberto 2017; Rello & Baeza-Yates 2016). The British Dyslexia Association's own guidance recommends plain sans-serif and never names a special font. What actually helps dyslexic and low-vision readers is the same proven set: larger size, generous spacing, shorter measure, good contrast, left-aligned.

✓ WHAT HELPS — for everyone

Larger size · generous line & letter spacing · a shorter measure · good contrast · left-aligned, ragged right. Doubling line/word spacing raised low-vision reading speed ~26–46% and more than halved errors.

Source: Wery & Diliberto 2017; Rello & Baeza-Yates 2016; British Dyslexia Association style guide; Blackmore-Wright 2013 (PLoS ONE).
