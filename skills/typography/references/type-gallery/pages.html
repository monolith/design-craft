Font kits — up to three typefaces that work together, in full pages

Five curated kits, each one to three screen-readable, free/open typefaces that pair well, shown in a complete sample page styled with a palette from the graphical-perception-and-color skill — then a sixth page where the type does real work inside Tufte charts and tables. Each kit card lists the fonts and the job each does — display (voice/headings), body (reading), UI (labels), mono (code/data). The rule throughout (§4): pair by contrast, make the body face the anchor, and use the fewest faces that do the job — two is plenty, three is the ceiling, and one is often enough. The combinations are a principled curation (taste built on the research), not proven-best — no pairing has a controlled winner.

1 · The superfamily — IBM Plex Sans · Serif · Mono

Lowest-risk way to use three faces: one type system designed to harmonise. The serif gives an editorial voice, the sans does the reading and UI, the mono carries code and data. Best for technical products, docs, and dashboards. Message: precise, engineered, trustworthy.

The quiet drift of the size-8 garment

By Maren Holt · 06 · 27 · 2026 · 9 min read

A garment labelled "size 8" has quietly grown for decades. The number on the tag stayed put; the tape measure did not. The story of that drift is, in miniature, the story of how a standard erodes when no one in particular is accountable for keeping it.

"The tag is a promise. Somewhere along the way, the promise stopped being kept."

Each individual adjustment was defensible. A label nudged half a centimetre to flatter; a competitor matched; a decade passed. No single decision broke the standard — the absence of anyone defending it did. The serif headline carries the voice; the body stays in a quiet sans so 9 minutes of reading never tires.

6 · Type inside charts & tables — the four skills composing

Where typography meets data. Faces: IBM Plex Sans (labels) · Plex Mono (numbers) — Plex is IBM Carbon's own data-viz anchor.
