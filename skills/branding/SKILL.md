# Branding (evidence-based visual identity)

## Overview

Most brand-design advice is folklore, agency blog posts, or extrapolated taste. A smaller body is
backed by controlled experiments and published marketing-science studies. This skill is that backed
set — findings from peer-reviewed journals (JM, JMR, JCR, JAMS, Journal of Consumer Psychology,
Psychology & Marketing), each with a named study and a confidence label.

**Core principle:** Fix the communication *objective* before the form, and choose by *fit* — to the
objective, the brand's personality, the product category, and the audience's familiarity — not by a
universal "best." Almost every real finding here is conditional: the same design dimension
(naturalness, descriptiveness, symmetry, a hue, a saturation level) reverses direction across
objectives and contexts. "Always do X" claims are where the myths live.

**Confidence labels:**
- **[STRONG]** — controlled experiment(s), primary-journal source, and confirmed 3-of-3 in this
  skill's adversarial research pass. Where a [STRONG] finding rests on a single paper, it is marked
  **[STRONG, single-source]** — top-tier and verified, but not yet meta-analytically replicated.
- **[ESTABLISHED]** — a canonical, heavily-cited peer-reviewed finding included from the literature
  but **not** run through this skill's adversarial verifier (see *Provenance* below). Stated
  conceptually — direction and downstream effect only, no extracted effect sizes. Used for two kinds
  of claim: the two emotional mechanisms a verification run could not confirm — brand experience and
  processing fluency (§I3–§I4) — and four findings **extracted** from the research corpus in a later
  gap analysis but never submitted to the verifier (logo shape §C′, the further Aaker critiques in §H,
  typeface personality §D′, handwritten-type attachment §I5), each flagged *(extracted in the research
  pass, not cross-examined here)*. Treat as a strong prior, one notch below [STRONG] only because *we*
  didn't cross-examine it.
- **[CONVENTION]** — widely taught, weak or mixed empirical support.
- **[MYTH]** — the evidence contradicts a popular claim.

**Provenance (read once).** The logo (§A–F), colour/personality (§G–H), and most of the emotional
layer (§I1–§I2) come from three adversarial research passes that fetched primary journals, extracted
claims, and required a 3-of-3 verifier vote. A dedicated third run verified brand attachment and brand
love to [STRONG]. Two emotional mechanisms — brand experience and processing fluency (§I3–§I4) —
produced zero surviving claims across the runs and stay [ESTABLISHED] (sourced from the canonical
literature, not cross-examined here); a future pass could lift them too. A later gap analysis added
four more **[ESTABLISHED]** findings — logo shape (§C′), the two further Aaker critiques (§H), typeface
personality (§D′), and handwritten-type attachment (§I5) — which were *extracted* from that corpus but
never themselves run through the verifier.

**Full research trail:** the distilled cited findings from the three runs are in
[references/branding-research.md](references/branding-research.md); the complete raw per-agent corpus — 72 source
extractions + 195 adversarial verifications, verbatim — is in [references/branding-corpus.md](references/branding-corpus.md).

**Hard floors (never yield).**
1. **Accessibility & contrast** — defer to **[[graphical-perception-and-color]]**. "Desaturated reads
   as premium" (§G6) and black-and-white-for-status never license failing WCAG AA text contrast.
2. **Legibility** — never drop below legibility standards. The incomplete-typeface trade-off
   (trust ↓, innovation ↑, §D) is a *deliberate, scoped* choice, not a loophole.
3. **Chart honesty** — **[[charting]]** governs data displays; branding never overrides data-ink discipline.

## A. Logo recognition framework — Henderson & Cote (1998) [STRONG]

**Source:** Henderson, P. W., & Cote, J. A. (1998). Guidelines for selecting or modifying logos.
*Journal of Marketing*, 62(2), 14–30.

Derived empirically: 195 real logos, each rated on **13 design characteristics** (naturalness,
harmony, elaborateness, symmetry, etc.), then mapped to three distinct communication objectives.

- **Recognition profile** — to maximise *accurate* recognition, a logo should be **very natural**,
  **very harmonious**, and **moderately elaborate** (an optimum in the middle, not the maximum).
  > *"High-recognition logos should be very natural, very harmonious, and moderately elaborate."*
- **Low-investment familiarity profile** — to create a feeling of knowing and positive affect
  *without* heavy media spend, a logo should be **less natural** (more abstract) and very harmonious
  — the *opposite* direction on naturalness.
  > *"Low-investment logos should be less natural and very harmonious."*

**Implication:** there is no single best logo form. Optimise for the objective first; naturalness
pulls opposite ways across objectives. Falsifies "more representational always wins" *and* "abstract
always works."

## B. Logo descriptiveness — Luffarelli, Mukesh & Mahmood (2019) [STRONG]

**Source:** Luffarelli, J., Mukesh, M., & Mahmood, A. (2019). Let the logo do the talking: the
influence of logo descriptiveness on brand equity. *Journal of Marketing Research*, 56(5), 862–878.

A logo is *descriptive* when its elements clearly communicate what the brand sells (an apple for a
fruit brand; a lock for security).

- **Effect:** more descriptive logos → better brand evaluations, purchase intentions, and brand
  performance.
- **Mechanism:** *descriptiveness → processing fluency → perceived authenticity → equity*. Easier to
  process ⇒ feels more authentic ⇒ consumers value that.
  > *"More descriptive logos are easier to process and thus elicit stronger impressions of
  > authenticity, which consumers value."*

**Boundary conditions — when descriptive does NOT win:**

| Condition | Effect |
|---|---|
| Brand *unfamiliar* | Positive |
| Brand *familiar* | Considerably attenuated |
| Category has *positive* associations | Positive |
| Category has *negative* associations | **Reverses** — descriptive hurts |

A pest-control logo that vividly depicts a cockroach is maximally descriptive and triggers the
negative-valence reversal. **Rule:** descriptive as a default only for unfamiliar brands in
neutral/positive categories; audit it for familiar brands; restrain it where the category content
carries negative affect.

## C. Logo symmetry × brand personality — Luffarelli, Stamatogiannakis & Yang (2019) [STRONG]

**Source:** *Journal of Marketing Research*, 56(1), 86–103.

- **Effect:** asymmetrical logos are perceived as **more arousing** → raise perceptions of **brand
  excitement**.
- **Fit effect ("visual asymmetry effect"):** asymmetry reads as congruent with an *exciting*
  personality; that fit lifts both consumer evaluations and the market's financial valuation.
  > *"Consumers perceive asymmetrical logos as more congruent with brands that have an exciting
  > personality … boosting evaluations and financial valuations."*
- **Boundary:** holds **only** for the *excitement* dimension and **only** for asymmetry.

**Rule:** exciting brand → asymmetry is evidence-backed. Every other personality → symmetry is the
default. Do not generalise "asymmetry is better" beyond excitement.

*(The evaluation leg is experimental; the "boosts financial valuations" leg rests on
archival/correlational BrandAsset/Interbrand data — causality is established only in the lab
evaluation studies.)*

## C′. Logo shape — circular vs angular attribute-congruence — Jiang, Gorn, Galli & Chattopadhyay (2016) [ESTABLISHED]

*(Extracted in the research pass, not cross-examined here. Fills the skill's "shape" scope, which §C
covers only for symmetry and §E only for redesign.)*

**Source:** Jiang, Y., Gorn, G. J., Galli, M., & Chattopadhyay, A. (2016). Does your company have the
right logo? *Journal of Consumer Research*, 42(5), 709–726.

- **Effect:** **circular** logos raise *soft* attribute judgments (e.g. comfortableness); **angular**
  logos raise *hard* ones (e.g. durability). Each shape moves **only its matching attribute** — not a
  general halo.
- **No liking advantage either way:** there is **no overall difference in logo liking or brand
  attitude** between the shapes (Exp 1, *F* < 1). Neither shape is "better" in the abstract.
- **Congruence gate — and reversal:** the effect on brand attitude and willingness-to-pay is
  **conditional on fit with the verbal positioning, and reverses**: a *durability* message → angular
  wins; a *comfort* message → circular wins.
- **Mechanism:** effortful visual imagery — the effect **disappears under visual (imagery) load**,
  confirming it runs through deliberate mental picturing rather than automatic affect.

**Rule:** pick shape to match the attribute the positioning promises (soft→circular, hard→angular);
do not expect either shape to lift liking on its own; a shape that fights the verbal message backfires.

## D. Incomplete typeface logos — Hagtvedt (2011) [STRONG]

**Source:** Hagtvedt, H. (2011). The impact of incomplete typeface logos on perceptions of the firm.
*Journal of Marketing*, 75(4), 86–93.

Logos whose characters are intentionally incomplete (parts blanked/occluded). Across three studies:

- Incomplete typeface **lowers** perceived **trustworthiness** — mediated by perceived *lack of clarity*.
- Incomplete typeface **raises** perceived **innovativeness** — mediated by perceived *interestingness*.
  > *"Although incomplete typeface logos have an unfavorable influence on perceived firm
  > trustworthiness, they have a favorable influence on perceived firm innovativeness."*

**Rule:** use only when innovativeness is a primary goal *and* trust is not load-bearing (consumer
tech, fashion, creative). Avoid for finance, healthcare, legal, or anywhere trust is the equity driver.

## D′. Typeface → brand personality — Henderson, Giese & Cote (2004) [ESTABLISHED]

*(Extracted in the research pass, not cross-examined here. This is the brand-personality layer of type;
face selection — size, measure, pairing, web-font loading — is owned by **[[typography]]**.)*

**Sources:** Henderson, P. W., Giese, J. L., & Cote, J. A. (2004). Impression management using typeface
design. *Journal of Marketing*, 68(4), 60–72; Grohmann, Giese & Parkman (2013), *J. Brand Management*.

- **Effect:** six typeface **design** dimensions (e.g. elaborateness, harmony, naturalness, flourish,
  weight, compression) map to four **impression** dimensions — **pleasing, engaging, reassuring,
  prominent**. Type choice is a deliberate personality lever, not decoration.
- **Strongest drivers:** **naturalness, harmony, and flourish** carry the most impression weight.
- **New brands:** for a *new* brand with no prior associations, **naturalness** is the single key
  driver of a favourable impression.

**Rule:** treat the typeface as a personality instrument — choose design dimensions to hit the target
impression (naturalness/harmony for warmth and approval), and lean hardest on naturalness for a new
brand. Hand the *selection* mechanics to **[[typography]]**.

## E. Logo redesign risk — Walsh, Winterich & Mittal (2011) [STRONG]

**Source:** Walsh, M. F., Winterich, K. P., & Mittal, V. (2011). Do logo redesigns help or hurt your
brand? The role of brand commitment. *Journal of Consumer Marketing*, 28(6), 438–447.

- **Effect:** higher brand commitment → more *negative* evaluation of an inconsistent (angular→rounded)
  redesign, and that negative logo evaluation **mediates** the hit to brand attitude. Commitment is a
  *negative* moderator.
  > *"Brand commitment negatively influences evaluation of inconsistent information (a rounded logo),
  > and this negative logo evaluation mediates the impact on brand attitude."*
- **Interpretation:** this is about *inconsistency*, not shape supremacy. It is **not** evidence that
  rounded < angular; the effect is contingent on prior commitment and self-construal.

**Rules:** measure commitment before redesigning; for high-commitment brands budget for an attitude
hit and stage the transition; never read this as "angular beats rounded."

## F. Logo complexity & processing fluency — Janiszewski & Meyvis (2001) [STRONG]

**Source:** Janiszewski, C., & Meyvis, T. (2001). Effects of brand logo complexity, repetition, and
spacing on processing fluency and judgment. *Journal of Consumer Research*, 28(1), 18–32.
*(Corrects an earlier draft that misattributed this finding to Pieters/Wedel/Zhang.)*

- **Effect:** logo **complexity**, repetition count, and exposure **spacing** *interact* to set how
  much fluency a given exposure level yields. A complex logo needs more (and better-spaced) exposures
  to reach the same fluency as a simple one.
  > *"Stimulus characteristics and presentation factors interact with repetition to determine the
  > amount of processing fluency … at various levels of exposure."*
- **Implication:** logo complexity is a **media-budget multiplier**. Simple logos generate fluency
  cheaply; complex logos demand investment in exposure and spacing. Constrained budget → prefer
  lower complexity.

*(Fluency is the connective mechanism running under §B, §D, and §I — see §I4.)*

## G. Brand colour

Brand-colour findings verified to [STRONG]. All defer to **[[graphical-perception-and-color]]** for
contrast, CVD-safety, and palette construction — these govern *meaning/personality*, never the
accessibility floor.

### G1. Colour is three-dimensional, not just hue [STRONG]
**Labrecque & Milne (2012), *JAMS* 40(5):711–727; Labrecque (2020), *Psychology & Marketing*.**
Brand personality depends on **hue, saturation, and value** — e.g. high saturation raised excitement;
high value reduced ruggedness; low saturation related to sincerity/sophistication. Rigorous colour
work must specify and control all three. "Blue = competent" is incomplete without its saturation/value.

### G2. Hue → personality, directionally [STRONG]
**Labrecque & Milne (2012).** Red → **excitement**; blue → **competence/trust**; also yellow →
excitement, white → sincerity, black → sophistication. Manipulating colour shifted both perceived
personality *and* purchase intent (strongest when the colour-induced personality matched preference).
The mapping rests on **associative learning** — directional and contextual, *not* a hue-only universal law.

### G3. Colour meaning is mostly learned (referential) [STRONG]
**Labrecque, Patrick & Milne (2013), *Psychology & Marketing* 30(2):187–202; Kimura et al. (2013),
*PLoS ONE*.** Meaning splits into **embodied** (intrinsic) vs **referential** (learned). Brand-specific
colour effects are predominantly *referential*: a "memory-colour effect" (perception shifting toward a
brand's true colour) appeared **only for high-familiarity logos** and scaled with the brand's real
reach. The embodied/innate side is the weaker-evidenced one for branding (a competing "colour adds
value without prior learning" claim was refuted 0-3).

### G4. Appropriateness is congruity, and incongruity can win [STRONG]
**Bottomley & Doyle (2006), *Marketing Theory* 6(1):63–84; Lyons & Wien (2017), *Food Quality and
Preference* 64:103–110.** Functional products are judged more appropriate in "functional" colours,
sensory-social products in "sensory-social" colours; knowing a brand's intended positioning shifts
which colours read as appropriate. **But** Lyons & Wien show a clean reversal: for *hedonic* products
colour-message *congruency* raises premiumness, while for *utilitarian* products *incongruency* does.
So "always pick the congruent colour" is false. *(The Lyons & Wien reversal rests on a single,
food-specific study — code it **[moderate — single study]**, not [STRONG] meta-analytic. The Bottomley
& Doyle congruity effect is the [STRONG] core of this section.)*

### G5. Differentiate-vs-conform is conditional [STRONG; refutes a MYTH]
**Labrecque & Milne (2013), *Marketing Letters* 24(2):165–176; Garaus & Halkias (2020), *Review of
Managerial Science* 14(5).** Differentiating your brand colour from category norms helps in some
categories and **hurts** in others. *Conforming* benefits brands in categories with a **dominant
market leader**, especially **high-involvement** ones; category-atypical colour raised consumer
*skepticism* and lowered interest. Refutes the folklore that a distinctive/ownable colour always wins.
*(The "differentiation harms" half is archival/correlational; the skepticism mechanism is experimental.)*

### G6. Desaturated / black-and-white → status [STRONG, single-source]
**Yue & Xia (2026), *J. Retailing & Consumer Services* 88; Zhou, Xiao, Yoon & Zhu (2025), *JCR*.**
Black-and-white logos raise perceived **status** (via perceived *coolness*); lower saturation raises
luxury status (via perceived *continuity/heritage*). Both are **conditional** — attenuated by
familiarity/warmth, and the saturation effect **reverses** (higher saturation better) under an
"innovative" positioning. *Recent (2025–26), not yet independently replicated.* **Never** let this
override the contrast floor.

### G7. The "80%" recognition figure is a [MYTH]
"Colour increases brand recognition by up to 80%" (attributed to a "Loyola" study) has **no traceable
controlled-study source**. Treat as folklore. The defensible substitute is G3: brand-colour
associations are *learned* and strengthen with exposure. (A separate "well-liked colours improve
recall" claim was refuted 0-3 — do not codify it either.)

## H. Brand personality — Aaker, and its validity flaw [STRONG]

**Sources:** Aaker, J. L. (1997). Dimensions of brand personality. *JMR*, 34(3), 347–356 *(the
taxonomy)*; Geuens, Weijters & De Wulf (2009), *IJRM* 26(2):97–107 *(validated alternative)*;
Azoulay & Kapferer (2003), *J. Brand Management* 11:143–155 *(critique origin)*.

- **The working vocabulary:** Aaker's five dimensions — **sincerity, excitement, competence,
  sophistication, ruggedness** — are the standard way to name a brand's personality, and the rest of
  this skill uses them (e.g. "exciting" in §C, §G2).
- **The flaw [STRONG critique]:** Aaker's scale has a documented construct-validity problem — its loose
  definition admits user-imagery/demographic descriptors (*feminine, upper-class, young*) that human
  Big-Five scales deliberately exclude. So an Aaker "reading" of a brand **partly measures the
  stereotyped user, not the brand**. Geuens et al. (2009; 12,789 respondents, 193 brands) built a
  personality-*only* 12-item scale (Activity, Responsibility, Aggressiveness, Simplicity, Emotionality),
  reliable and cross-culturally valid.
- **Two further documented critiques [ESTABLISHED]** *(Austin, Siguaw & Mattila 2003; Aaker,
  Benet-Martínez & Garolera 2001 — extracted in the research pass, not cross-examined here):*
  - **Aggregate-only:** the five-factor structure holds *across* brands but **does not hold at the
    individual-brand level** — precisely the per-brand "reading" this skill invokes. A five-dimension
    profile is safe as category-level vocabulary, shaky as one brand's fingerprint.
  - **Cross-cultural non-replication:** across US / Japan / Spain only **Sincerity, Excitement, and
    Sophistication** recur; **Ruggedness is US-specific** and **Competence is absent in Spain**. So
    "ruggedness" (used as a working dimension in §G1) is not a safe cross-context lever.

**Rule:** use Aaker's dimensions as the shared language for targeting personality; when you need a
*rigorous* measurement (research, A/B, valuation), prefer Geuens et al. and remember the user-stereotype
confound. Treat a *single brand's* five-factor profile, and any use of "ruggedness"/"competence"
outside the US, as provisional.

## I. Emotional mechanisms — why identity design bonds

> **Provenance:** §I1–§I2 are adversarially verified **[STRONG]** (a dedicated third research run,
> 104 agents, 3-of-3 votes). §I3–§I4 (brand experience, processing fluency) produced zero surviving
> claims across the runs and stay **[ESTABLISHED]** — canonical literature, not cross-examined here.
> §I5 (handwritten type → attachment) is **[ESTABLISHED]**, extracted in the gap-analysis pass and
> likewise not cross-examined here. This is the layer that explains *why* the §A–H rules move equity,
> and what they are ultimately building toward.

### I1. Brand attachment — Thomson, MacInnis & Park (2005); Park et al. (2010) [STRONG]
*"The ties that bind,"* J. Consumer Psychology 15(1):77–91; *"Brand attachment and brand attitude
strength,"* Journal of Marketing 74(6):1–17.
- **Emotional attachment** is the emotion-laden bond to a *specific* brand, measured by a validated
  10-item scale on three facets — **affection, passion, connection**. In a model containing rival
  predictors it was the **only** significant driver of willingness to pay a price premium (b ≈ 0.26)
  *beyond* attitude, satisfaction, and involvement, and it predicted loyalty — so attachment is **not**
  a relabelling of attitude or satisfaction (discriminant validity confirmed).
- **Attachment as a self-bond** (Park et al. 2010): the strength of the bond connecting the brand to
  the **self**, with two factors — brand-self **connection** and brand **prominence**. It is a
  **stronger predictor of *actual* behaviour** — real purchase, brand purchase share, need share — than
  brand-*attitude* strength, and is largely **time-dependent** (an advanced relationship stage), whereas
  strong attitudes can form quickly.
- **Design implication:** attachment is the construct uniquely shown to drive **price premium and actual
  market-share behaviour**. Identity work that deepens self-connection and keeps the brand prominent
  (top-of-mind) is aimed here — not at mere liking.

### I2. Brand love — Batra, Ahuvia & Bagozzi (2012) [STRONG]
*"Brand Love,"* *Journal of Marketing*, 76(2):1–16. Brand love is a **higher-order prototype** construct
(not a single emotion) of seven elements: self-brand integration, passion-driven behaviours, positive
emotional connection, long-term relationship, positive overall attitude, certainty/confidence, and
anticipated separation distress. Its antecedent is **perceived quality**; its downstream signature is a
*joint* **loyalty + word-of-mouth + resistance-to-negative-information** outcome.
- **Misattribution to avoid:** brand love does **not** include willingness-to-pay — it was excluded for
  poor reliability. Don't promise a price premium from "brand love"; price premium is *attachment's*
  outcome (§I1), not love's.

### I3. Brand experience — Brakus, Schmitt & Zarantonello (2009) [ESTABLISHED]
*"Brand Experience,"* *Journal of Marketing*, 73(3):52–68. Brand experience is evoked across four
dimensions — **sensory, affective, intellectual, behavioural** — and affects satisfaction and loyalty.
Visual identity is the most direct lever on the *sensory* and *affective* dimensions.

### I4. Processing fluency — Reber, Schwarz & Winkielman (2004) [ESTABLISHED]
*"Processing fluency and aesthetic pleasure,"* *Personality & Social Psychology Review*, 8(4):364–382.
Stimuli that are **easier to process** are liked more. This is the cross-cutting mechanism beneath the
verified rules: descriptiveness (§B), moderate-not-maximal complexity (§A, §F), and colour-category
congruity (§G4) all work by *raising fluency*. When the §A–H rules conflict, "which choice is easier
to process for this audience?" is the tie-breaker the established literature supports — though, unlike
§I1–§I2, fluency itself was not confirmed in this skill's runs.

### I5. Handwritten typeface → humanization → attachment — Schroll & Schnurr (2018) [ESTABLISHED]
*(Extracted in the research pass, not cross-examined here. A type→attachment bridge; face selection is
owned by **[[typography]]**.)* *"Humanizing products with handwritten typefaces,"* *Journal of Consumer
Research*, 45(3).
- **Effect:** **handwritten** (vs machine-written) type signals **human presence**, which raises
  **emotional attachment** (§I1), which lifts favourable evaluation. Reported field experiment: **30.4%
  vs 5.6%** purchase; a 12-study internal meta gives **g ≈ .28**.
- **Bounded / reversing:** the lift is **not universal** — machine-written type wins for *functionally*
  positioned products, and there is **no lift for brands consumers are already attached to**. It is a
  **new-brand / relationship-building** tool, not a default.
- **Design implication:** reach for handwritten type when the goal is warmth and a *forming* bond on an
  *emotionally* positioned brand; keep machine type where the promise is function or the bond already
  exists.

### The ladder (synthesis) [STRONG]
The emotional constructs form a differentiated hierarchy, not synonyms: **satisfaction / brand attitude**
(most cognitive, the weakest behavioural predictor) → **attachment** (the self-bond; the only rung shown
to drive price-premium WTP and actual market share) → **brand love** (the broadest — it absorbs attitude
valence, attitude strength, and separation distress, and drives loyalty/advocacy). Target the rung that
matches the goal: **attachment** for price premium and share, **brand love** for advocacy and resilience.

## Process — designing a brand's visual identity

A repeatable sequence that orders the verified findings. Each step names its section.

1. **Fix the objective first** (§A). Accurate recognition? Cheap familiarity/affect? High image?
   The objective sets naturalness and elaboration — and they pull opposite ways, so you cannot skip this.
2. **Name the target personality** (§H). Use Aaker's five as the vocabulary; note the user-stereotype
   caveat. "Exciting" specifically unlocks the asymmetry option in step 4.
3. **Choose logo form** (§A–§F):
   - Naturalness & elaboration per the objective (§A) — moderate elaboration for recognition.
   - Symmetry by default; asymmetry **only** for exciting brands (§C).
   - Descriptiveness as a default only for *unfamiliar* brands in *non-negative* categories (§B).
   - Treat complexity as a media-budget multiplier (§F) — simpler if exposure budget is thin.
   - Incomplete typeface only when innovation outranks trust (§D).
4. **Choose colour** (§G), specifying **all three** dimensions (§G1):
   - Pick hue for the target personality (§G2), remembering it's learned/contextual.
   - Judge appropriateness by product type & positioning (§G4) — and consider a deliberate
     *incongruent* colour for utilitarian products.
   - Decide differentiate-vs-conform by category structure (§G5): conform if there's a dominant
     leader, especially high-involvement.
   - Reach for desaturation/B&W only when *status* is the goal (§G6) — and never below the contrast floor.
5. **If redesigning** (§E): measure brand commitment first; committed audiences punish inconsistency;
   stage the change.
6. **Pressure-test for fluency** (§I4): of the choices that survive, prefer the one that is *easiest to
   process* for the intended audience — that is the literature-backed tie-breaker, and the route to
   attachment (§I1–§I2).
7. **Clear the floors** (Hard floors, top): WCAG contrast, legibility, chart honesty. These override
   any step above.

## Myths summary

| Claim | Status | Correct picture |
|---|---|---|
| "More elaboration always aids recognition" | [MYTH] | *Moderate* elaboration optimises recognition; maximal does not (§A). |
| "Descriptive logos always win" | [MYTH] | Attenuated for familiar brands; *reverses* for negative-valence categories (§B). |
| "More symmetry is always better" | [MYTH] | Asymmetry helps *only* exciting brands; symmetry is the default otherwise (§C). |
| "Rounded logos always win" | [MYTH] | It's about *consistency* with prior identity, not shape supremacy (§E). |
| "More exposure = more liking" | [MYTH] | Fluency depends on complexity × repetition × spacing — no simple exposure-count rule (§F). |
| "Pick the colour that means your trait" | [MYTH] | The naive one-hue-one-trait rule fails: hue meaning is learned & contextual, runs on all three colour dimensions, and is a *congruity* effect (§G1–G4). |
| "An ownable, distinctive colour always helps" | [MYTH] | Conform to category norms when there's a dominant leader; differentiation can hurt (§G5). |
| "Colour increases recognition by 80%" | [MYTH] | No peer-reviewed study supports the figure (§G7). |
