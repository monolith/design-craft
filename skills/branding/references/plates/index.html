design-craft · branding

Reference plates

Each plate isolates one design variable on an invented mark to show a published finding. The marks are didactic illustrations — not tested stimuli. The citation beneath each plate is the evidence; the drawing only shows the contrast it describes.

Logo form
01

Elaboration has a middle optimum

STRONG

For accurate recognition the prescribed profile is very natural, very harmonious, and moderately elaborate — the elaboration optimum sits in the middle, not at the maximum. (Complexity is also a media-cost multiplier — see plate 07.) Henderson & Cote (1998), Journal of Marketing 62(2):14–30. [STRONG]

02

Naturalness has no single best — it depends on the objective

STRONG

The naturalness optimum reverses by objective: a mark built for accurate recognition should be very natural, but one built to manufacture a feeling of familiarity and positive affect without heavy media spend should be less natural. No single form wins — pick the objective first. Henderson & Cote (1998), Journal of Marketing 62(2):14–30. [STRONG]

03

Descriptive logos help — until the category turns negative

STRONG

More descriptive logos lift evaluations, purchase intent and performance — via easier processing → perceived authenticity — but only for unfamiliar brands in non-negative categories. For a category with contaminating associations (the literal pest for a pest-control brand) the effect reverses and descriptiveness hurts. Luffarelli, Mukesh & Mahmood (2019), Journal of Marketing Research 56(5):862–878. [STRONG]

04

Asymmetry reads as excitement — but only there

STRONG

Asymmetrical logos are perceived as more arousing, raising perceptions of brand excitement, and the design–personality fit lifts both consumer evaluations and financial valuation. The effect holds only for the excitement dimension and only for asymmetry — for every other personality, symmetry is the default. Luffarelli, Stamatogiannakis & Yang (2019), Journal of Marketing Research 56(1):86–103. [STRONG]

05

Incomplete typefaces trade trust for innovation

STRONG

Across three studies, intentionally incomplete typeface logos lower perceived trustworthiness (via reduced clarity) and raise perceived innovativeness (via interestingness). A real trade-off, not a style — use only when innovation outranks trust, never where trust is the equity driver (finance, healthcare, legal). Hagtvedt (2011), Journal of Marketing 75(4):86–93. [STRONG]

06

Redesign risk is about consistency, not shape

STRONG

Higher brand commitment produces more negative evaluation of an inconsistent redesign, and that mediates a hit to brand attitude. The cost is the inconsistency with an established identity — not angular-versus-rounded. Measure commitment before redesigning; stage the change. Walsh, Winterich & Mittal (2010), J. Product & Brand Management 19(2):76–84. [STRONG]

07

Complexity is a media-budget multiplier

STRONG

Logo complexity, repetition and spacing interact to set how much fluency a given exposure level yields: a complex mark needs more — and better-spaced — impressions to reach the same familiarity as a simple one. If the media budget is thin, prefer lower complexity. Janiszewski & Meyvis (2001), Journal of Consumer Research 28(1):18–32. [STRONG]

Brand colour · defers to graphical-perception-and-color for contrast & CVD
08

Hue carries personality — directionally

STRONG

Hue maps directionally onto brand personality — red → excitement, blue → competence/trust. The mapping is learned and contextual, not a universal law. Labrecque & Milne (2012), J. of the Academy of Marketing Science 40(5):711–727. [STRONG]

09

It is not just hue — saturation and value shift personality too

STRONG

One hue, three readings: brand personality rides on all three colour dimensions, so "blue = competent" is incomplete without its saturation and value. Specify hue + saturation + value — high saturation amplified excitement; lighter, less-saturated colours read more sincere and sophisticated. Labrecque & Milne (2012); Labrecque (2020), Psychology & Marketing. [STRONG]

10

Colour appropriateness is congruity, not an absolute

STRONG

A colour is not appropriate in the abstract — it is judged relative to the product and positioning. A functional product (here a gear) reads "right" in a functional colour and off in a sensory-social one. The twist: for utilitarian framings incongruent colour can actually win — so "always match" is false too. Bottomley & Doyle (2006), Marketing Theory 6(1):63–84; Lyons & Wien (2017). [STRONG]

11

A distinctive colour does not always win

STRONG

Breaking from category colour norms helps in some categories and hurts in others. Conforming tends to win where the category has a dominant market leader (especially high-involvement), and category-atypical colour can raise consumer skepticism. The ownable-distinctive-colour rule is conditional, not universal. Labrecque & Milne (2013), Marketing Letters 24(2); Garaus & Halkias (2020). [STRONG]

12

Desaturated and black-and-white signal status

STRONG

Black-and-white logos raise perceived status (via coolness) and lower saturation raises luxury status (via continuity/heritage) — both conditionally, and the saturation effect reverses under an "innovative" positioning. This never licenses failing the contrast floor. Yue & Xia (2026), J. Retailing & Consumer Services 88; Zhou et al. (2025), JCR. [STRONG, single-source]

Colour contrast, CVD-safety and palette construction defer to graphical-perception-and-color; type to typography. Where a plate holds colour dimensions fixed (08, 09, 12) the stated saturation/value are matched so only the named variable moves. These plates illustrate findings from the cited studies — they are not measurements of these particular marks.
