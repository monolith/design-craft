# Composition & Design Theory

## Overview

The **arrangement / composition specialist** of design-craft. The sibling skills each own one lever — colour, type, chart
forms, screen layout. This skill owns the **composition**: what goes where, at what size, in what
relationship, so the whole field reads as one deliberate, meaningful piece. It brings composition and
design-theory judgment to a brief and directs the other skills to supply their levers.

Distilled from a research pass on graphic-design theory and ~17 masters (the synthesis:
[`references/composition-research.md`](references/composition-research.md); the raw per-agent corpus — 29 source
extractions + 75 adversarial verifications, verbatim — in [`references/composition-corpus.md`](references/composition-corpus.md)); a worked gallery of 41 plates
applies it: [`references/gallery/index.html`](references/gallery/index.html).

**Core principle — meaning is in the arrangement, not the inventory.** Three identical lines become a
triangle, an "H," or a table depending only on how they are joined. So compose the whole field as one
structured system first, then place the marks — and never let a medium's defaults (above all, the
webpage's) decide the form.

**The two moves — fusion and juxtaposition (the spine of this skill).** Arranging principles *is* the craft,
and there are exactly two ways to do it. **Fusion (the *mix*)** — principles *compound* into one unified
effect; many lenses resolve to a single look (a tiny module + gestalt grouping + a generative rule become one
swarm). **Juxtaposition** — principles are set *in tension*, and the friction between them *is* the subject:
order against energy, clarity against mystery, the letter against its own void. Both still obey everything
below — one takeaway, salience on the subject, violate no principle — they differ only in whether the combined
principles **agree** (fusion) or **argue** (juxtaposition). **When to reach for which** — pick by what the takeaway *is*, none of the three a default: if it is **one
pure idea**, use that **single principle alone** — don't combine for the sake of it; if several principles
push **one unified quality**, **fuse** them into a single effect; if the takeaway is itself a *relationship* —
order vs chaos, before vs after, the one vs the many, anything you can't state without a "versus" or a "but" —
**juxtapose**, and make the friction the subject. **Name which move you are making before you
compose.** Most weak "combinations" are a fusion that should have been a juxtaposition, or two *ideas*
mistaken for one. The twelve principles are the vocabulary; fusion and juxtaposition are the grammar — read
the rest of this skill through them. *(Gallery cards 19–41 are built as exactly this split — ten fusions, thirteen juxtapositions; "Compounding, not
combining" below is the fusion deep-cut, the constraint rule its guardrail.)*

**The governing rule — serve one takeaway, effortlessly.** Before composing, name the *single thing* the
viewer must take away. Then every element must either advance that takeaway or be cut — design reinforces
the point; it is never there "for design's sake." **Design *helps* the message; it must not *become* the message** —
admired for its own cleverness — unless the artifact's subject genuinely *is* design (a type specimen, a
colour study); *content as form* is the one allowed fusion, where the design *carries* the message so
attention still lands on it, not the craft. If the eye has to *work* to figure out what it is looking
at — too many elements, competing focal points, decoration without purpose, an overloaded field — the
composition has failed, however handsome. This rule **governs the twelve principles below**: reduction,
hierarchy, and clarity are simply its instruments. *(Grounds: processing fluency — material that is easier
to process is judged clearer and trusted more; and Vignelli's pragmatics — "if it is not understood, it is
worthless.")*

**Content as form — let the point become the design.** The strongest composition doesn't *illustrate* the
message beside the layout; it makes the message *be* the layout. Ask which piece of the information can be
rendered so that it **is** the dominant visual — the data's own shape, the key number, the word itself —
instead of a chart or a caption set next to text. When the point transforms into the form, design and
takeaway arrive as one thing, and there is nothing extra to add or to parse. (This is principle 4, "type
is image," generalised to data and meaning — and the highest expression of the governing rule above.)
**Make this the first thing you try and the ceiling you aim for on every composition** — the strongest
work, the single-principle plates included, all reach it. If a brief truly cannot, fall back to a clean,
near-single-principle treatment rather than a busy merge.

**Salience serves the subject — aim the emphasis at what the takeaway is *about*.** The eye lands first on
the most salient mark — the figure, the one accent colour, the biggest/brightest/filled element — so that
mark must *be* the subject of the takeaway, never its complement. The frequent failure is the **absence
claim**: when the point is "almost none," "mostly un-X," or "what's missing," the eye is pulled to the small
thing that is *present* (the filled sliver, the lone data point) because that is the easy, concrete mark — so
the composition pops the opposite of its own headline. Give the dominance to the absence instead: make the
void the figure, spend the one reserved accent on the majority, let the small exception recede. This rule
*aims* figure-ground (principle 2) and hierarchy (principle 9), and it is where colour's "one reserved accent
on the thing that matters" gets pointed (→ `graphical-perception-and-color`). A clear hierarchy aimed at the
wrong element still fails. *(Grounds: preattentive pop-out — a single salient mark is found first and
fastest, so it must carry the message; Healey & Enns 2012, Treisman 1985. [STRONG])*

**Every principle is also a constraint — violate none, used or not.** The twelve are a kit you *choose*
levers from, but also a set you must not *break*: a piece may lean on two or three, yet it must still not run
*against* any of the others — including ones you never invoked. (The salience rule above is one instance —
figure-ground binds the piece even when it isn't your lever; #18's first version broke it.) Audit the whole
canon before shipping, not only what you used — this is the guardrail that lets you juxtapose freely.

**Confidence labels:** **[STRONG]** peer-reviewed study or normative standard · **[CONVENTION]** canonical
practice / named authority, thin empirical support · **[MYTH]** failed verification — do not use.

**Honest premise.** Design is teachable and the findings here are real *where labelled* [STRONG]; that
following the skill *guarantees* a better result is not itself proven. Treat it as disciplined craft, not a
formula — the labels mark where the ground is firm.

## When to use

- Composing any artifact where arrangement carries meaning: posters, identities, covers, slides, key art,
  editorial, data reports, signage, screens.
- Art-directing across the other design-craft skills — decide the composition here, then hand type/colour/
  charts/layout to the skill that owns each.
- Diagnosing why a layout feels generic, cluttered, derivative, or like a "stock graphic."

Not for: the colour palette itself (→ `graphical-perception-and-color`), typeface/legibility (→
`typography`), which chart form (→ `charting`), screen navigation/usability (→ `interface-ux-and-layout`),
inventing a whole new house style (→ `developing-style`).

## The method — how to compose

Build every composition in this order.

**Choose the move that fits the takeaway — on merit, not by default.** Three options are open: a **single
principle** (one pure idea, used alone), a **fusion** (principles compounding into one effect), or a
**juxtaposition** (principles in tension). The named combinations below are a ready-made palette of the
fusions and juxtapositions — reach for one when its *use-when* fits, since a proven move beats re-deriving —
but a single principle is often the best answer, and no combination beats a forced one. Pick what the
takeaway *is*; never bias toward tension for its own sake.

0. **Name the one takeaway** (the governing rule). What must the viewer leave with? Everything downstream
   exists to serve it.
1. **Primary principle = the one that most reinforces that takeaway.** Don't pick a principle because it's
   handsome — ask first: *which principle makes this takeaway land hardest, with least effort for the eye?*
   That one carries the piece. Ask too whether the point can **become the form** (content as form, above) —
   the strongest compositions fuse message and form into one thing; reach for a separate chart or caption
   only when it genuinely can't be done.
2. **Supporting principle = the next that reinforces the takeaway without adding noise.** It must
   strengthen the point, never introduce a second message or compete for dominance. Decide its **move**:
   does it *fuse* with the primary (compound one effect) or *juxtapose* against it (a chosen tension)? Both
   are valid — an *unintended* fight between principles is the bug; a *staged* one is the design.
3. **Layer only what still serves the takeaway.** Further moves — a minor principle, a deeper-cut finding,
   the sibling skills (colour, type, charts, layout) — earn a place *only* by reinforcing the point.
   Anything that adds a competing focus, decoration, or load is cut — **even if it is a perfectly valid
   principle.** More elements is the most common way to weaken a takeaway — but note *elements*, not
   *principles*: maximize the principles that compound (see "Compounding," below), minimize the marks that
   carry them. Restraint here is the difference between a composition and a pile.
4. **Clear the floors** (below): demonstrate-don't-reproduce, the medium-agnostic guard, and the
   accessibility/legibility floors the siblings own.
5. **Pass the 3-second test.** Look at it cold (or show someone): can the *single* takeaway be stated at a
   glance? Then name **where the eye lands first** — it must be the *subject* of that takeaway, not its
   complement. (A piece can pass "state the takeaway" because the words are legible, yet still send the eye
   to the wrong mark — the inverted-salience failure.) If either fails, you have merged *ideas* or mis-aimed
   the emphasis — cut or re-aim until both hold. This is the gate; a handsome composition that fails it has
   failed.

> **Compounding, not combining — the heart of synthesis.** A strong composition has exactly *one* idea and
> *one* hero, however many principles serve it. The single-principle plates work because idea and principle
> are 1:1; a good synthesis keeps the idea at **one** while letting principles *compound* on it to make it
> land harder. The failure mode is combining several *ideas* — that is two posters, not one. If a brief
> carries two messages, split it.
>
> *Maximize the principles, not the marks.* Bring as many principles to bear on the one idea as each can
> **earn** — a principle earns its place only when it makes the takeaway land *meaningfully* harder, never
> marginally. The catch: **compound theories, not elements.** Many principles ride on a *few* marks — one
> bar can serve figure-ground, hierarchy, reduction, and an index-sign at once — so richer thinking is not a
> busier surface. Juxtapose principles for productive tension wherever it sharpens the point, **provided the
> juxtaposition breaks none of them** (used or not — see the constraint rule above).

> The applied examples in the gallery (plates 13–18) put the method on a real brief: name the takeaway, find
> the mechanism that embodies it, then compose a fusion or a juxtaposition in service of the message — the
> skill at work, not self-referential.

**Subject ≠ driver.** Even when a sibling skill is the *subject* of a piece — a chart-led data report, a
type specimen, a colour study — the composition principles still **lead and dominate the design**. The
charts (or type, or colour) fill a composition that the twelve principles drive; they never *become* the
composition. Test: if the first thing the design does is "show a chart," the principle is not dominating
yet — raise a composition principle (usually **hierarchy** or **grid**) to carry the piece, and let the
chart sit inside it as the layered subject.

**A fresh take changes the form, not just the arrangement.** When a piece must read as genuinely *new*,
re-arranging the *same* core form — the same chart, the same structural device — still reads as the same
piece. Reconsider the form itself first, then compose. (Worked: plate 16 only felt fresh once the slopegraph
became a diverging change-bar; tidying the slopegraph's layout did not.)

## The twelve principles (choose your primary + supporting here)

1. **The grid** — divide the surface into fields; dispose every element in one rational system. Order as a
   stated attitude, not taste. *[CONVENTION/canon — Müller-Brockmann] · plate 01*
2. **Figure-ground** — which region reads as figure vs ground is *engineerable* (convexity, size,
   surroundedness, lower-region bias the eye). *[STRONG — Wagemans 2012] · plate 02*
3. **Diagonal = dynamism** — the right angle is static; tilt the axis and the field comes alive.
   *[CONVENTION — Lissitzky, stated] · plate 03*
4. **Type is image** — type can be the picture and carry the meaning, not just label it.
   *[CONVENTION — Scher] · plate 04*
5. **Generative vocabulary** — fix one small rule/module and derive a whole system from it.
   *[STRONG reduction lever — developing-style; Wyman, Fuerte] · plate 05*
6. **Meaning from context** — a neutral mark takes its meaning from placement and frame.
   *[STRONG — semiotics, Danesi/Barthes] · plate 06*
7. **Clarity vs mystery** — decide whether the piece states plainly or withholds to be decoded; rarely
   both at full volume. *[CONVENTION — Kidd] · plate 07*
8. **Reduction / active space** — emptiness is the field that gives a single mark its force.
   *[CONVENTION — Maeda, Sutnar, Vignelli] · plate 08*
9. **Hierarchy & contrast** — scale and weight set the order of reading; one dominant element (but
   **overlap/occlusion can outrank size** — see deeper cuts). *[STRONG — distinct hierarchy aids scanning + recall] · plate 09*
10. **Gestalt grouping** — proximity, similarity, and **closure** bind separate marks into wholes.
    *[STRONG — Wagemans 2012] · plate 10*
11. **Type that recedes** — for sustained reading, the letterform's duty is to disappear into even
    texture. *[STRONG legibility floor / CONVENTION — Garamond tradition] · plate 11*
12. **Sign-types (icon / index / symbol)** — a mark means by resemblance, by trace, or by convention.
    *[STRONG — Peirce] · plate 12*

## Named combinations — the first-reach palette

The twelve principles already worked into **23 named, repeatable moves** (built and demonstrated as gallery
cards 19–41). **Reach for one of these before composing from raw principles** — a named move is a proven start
and gives more original, less generic results than re-deriving each time. Each carries a *use-when* so you can
choose without working it out from scratch. **Match the move to the takeaway, not to a default** — a fusion
when several principles serve one unified quality, a juxtaposition when the takeaway is a contrast or
relationship, and neither when one pure principle (01–12) already says it. Tension is often *more original*
but not *always right*: forcing a juxtaposition onto a brand whose essence is restraint over-reaches (Google's
calm box wanted a fusion; Amazon's "one out of everything" wanted a juxtaposition).

**Fusions — principles compound into one effect** *(use for a single, unified quality)*
- **Ghost Grid** (19) · Grid + Reduction — *use when* order should be felt, not drawn: sparse, premium layouts where alignment alone implies the system.
- **The Carved Void** (20) · Figure-ground + Reduction — *use when* the subject is an absence, or a bold iconic figure should come from minimal means: the shape is what's removed.
- **The Monolith** (21) · Type-as-image + Reduction + Hierarchy — *use when* one word or number must dominate utterly: a single-word statement, a hero stat.
- **The Swarm** (22) · Generative + Gestalt — *use when* many small units should read as one larger form or quantity: emergence, scale, a crowd becoming a shape.
- **The Specimen** (23) · Generative + Grid — *use when* you must show a system or its full range: a catalogue, a type/pattern specimen.
- **Text as Texture** (24) · Type-that-recedes + Grid — *use when* body text should recede into calm grey texture and one element surfaces: editorial, reading-first pages.
- **The Mosaic Word** (25) · Type-as-image + Gestalt + Generative — *use when* "micro builds macro" is the point: a whole made of countless parts resolving to one legible hero form.
- **The Loaded Dot** (26) · Reduction + Meaning-from-context — *use when* one neutral mark must carry meaning from a sparse frame: a data point, "you are here," a single charged symbol.
- **The Glyph** (27) · Sign-types + Reduction + Generative — *use when* you need an essence-mark that looks systematically derived: logos, icons, a reduced symbol with rigor.
- **Letterform as Object** (39) · Type-as-image + Sign-types — *use when* a letter can double as the thing it names: a witty, memorable type/image pun for a mark or poster.

**Juxtapositions — principles in tension, the friction is the subject** *(prefer these; reach here first when the brief holds any contrast)*
- **Loud / Quiet** (28) · Type-as-image ✗ Type-that-recedes — *use when* one element must shout while the rest whispers: extreme emphasis, a single dominant message.
- **The Canted Grid** (29) · Grid ✗ Diagonal — *use when* order needs energy or unease: a rational system made restless (the *whole* grid tilted).
- **Hurled Type** (30) · Type-as-image ✗ Diagonal — *use when* type must carry motion or impact: launch, disruption, "thrown."
- **The Counterform** (31) · Type-as-image ✗ Figure-ground — *use when* the eye should flip between a form and its gap: ambiguity, a double reading.
- **The Chameleon Mark** (32) · Sign-types ✗ Meaning-from-context — *use when* one mark must mean different things by its frame: "context changes everything."
- **The Cascade** (33) · Diagonal ✗ Hierarchy — *use when* a ranked sequence needs downhill momentum: ordered flow that moves.
- **The Withheld** (34) · Clarity ✗ Mystery — *use when* you contrast stated vs concealed: a reveal, encryption, "the same thing, hidden."
- **Nested Order** (35) · Hierarchy ✗ Gestalt — *use when* one must stand out from an equal crowd: the singular vs the many, a leader or outlier.
- **The Cleave** (36) · Diagonal ✗ Figure-ground — *use when* a field splits into two opposed territories: before/after, this/that, a divide where figure becomes ground.
- **The Strike** (37) · Hierarchy + Diagonal ✗ Reduction — *use when* one bold mark in a void must carry maximum drama: a single decisive gesture, alarm.
- **Order & Accident** (38) · Grid ✗ Diagonal — *use when* one rogue breaks a system and becomes the hero: the exception, "one breaks rank" (upright grid, single intruder).
- **Thrust & Counter-thrust** (40) · Diagonal ✗ Diagonal — *use when* two opposing forces collide or balance: conflict, push-pull, two sides meeting.
- **Productive Dissonance** (41) · Meaning-from-context ✗ tone — *use when* meaning comes from a deliberate clash: a jarring intruder in a calm field, "wrongness on purpose."

## Deeper cuts — moves to layer in step 3

Not headline principles, but documented and useful once the pair is set:

- **Compose as one field** — Prägnanz (perception resolves to the simplest whole) + non-additivity (the
  whole ≠ the sum of parts). *[STRONG perception; the design reading is convention]*
- **Controlled visual flow** — route the eye deliberately (the upper-right corner, explicit starts and stops). *[CONVENTION — Sutnar]*
- **Overlap/occlusion outranks size for hierarchy** — when one element occludes another, the occluder reads as
  figure and ranks higher; a within-subjects study found layout *structure* (overlapping > tangential >
  separated) drove perceived hierarchy **more than size**, and proximity alone doesn't create hierarchy without a
  size difference. Grounds in figure-ground (#2) + closure (#10). *[extracted — one study, N=57, abstract shapes on a touchscreen; a bounded lever, not a law]*
- **Abstract to a timeless symbol** rather than illustrate literally. *[CONVENTION — Nitsche]*
- **Semantics → syntactics → pragmatics** — find the meaning, structure it, then test it is understood. *[CONVENTION — Vignelli]*
- **Meaning by appropriation** — recontextualise a charged found image; sometimes omit type to intensify it. *[CONVENTION — Saville]*
- **Fuse one structural rule with culturally specific style.** *[CONVENTION — Wyman]*
- **Organize: make many appear as few.** *[CONVENTION — Maeda]*
- **Design the behaviour/system, not a fixed artifact** — carry the caveat that motion/identity systems
  are screen/time-bound. *[CONVENTION — Dixon, Wolff Olins]*
- **Position deliberately against the prevailing convention** — the hand-off to developing-style.
  *[CONVENTION — Chwast; = developing-style's core]*
- **Treat the frame edge as active**, the surface as constructed space. *[CONVENTION — Lissitzky]*
- **Distil to one idea** — "a cover is a haiku." *[CONVENTION — Kidd]*
- **Thrust & counter-thrust** — set a directional force against a counter-force; opposed vectors energise the
  field and route the eye. A natural *juxtaposition*. *[CONVENTION — Beall]*
- **Productive dissonance** — pair an element with a contradictory counterpoint (a sedate image, an
  unsettling tone); the friction itself carries the meaning. A natural *juxtaposition*. *[CONVENTION — Saville]*
- **Letterform as object / visual pun** — let an object stand in for a letter, so type and image *fuse* into
  a single sign. *[CONVENTION — Pineles]*
- **Type lives on one axis: recede ↔ perform** — principles 4 and 11 are the two poles of one spectrum (the
  Garamond tradition ↔ Scher); where type sits on it is a semantic choice, and pitting the poles against each
  other is itself a juxtaposition (plate 28). *[CONVENTION — the type-as-meaning axis]*

## Floors — non-negotiable

**Demonstrate, don't reproduce.** Learn a master's *move*; never rebuild their specific famous work.
Copying a familiar exemplar causes design fixation — the opposite of originality. Credit the principle,
make something new.

**Medium-agnostic guard — don't let the webpage decide the form.** State the composition independent of
medium; map it to the medium last. Unless the artifact *is* a screen, do not default to hero-banner /
nav-bar / card-grid / centered-stack. And avoid the "stock graphic" trap — glows, faux-3D spheres, lens
flares, gradient sci-fi clip-art: prefer flat, intentional, honest form. It must hold up as art and print,
not just on a monitor.

**Accessibility & legibility** (owned by the siblings, binding here): AA contrast and palette roles →
`graphical-perception-and-color`; minimum sizes / measure / tabular figures → `typography`; chart honesty
/ data-ink → `charting`. Never relax these for effect.

## Making one — execute, then iterate

The method, principles, and floors above are how you *decide* and *judge*; this is how you *produce* — the
half a reference gallery can't teach. Three moves turn a decision into a finished piece, and every plate in
the gallery went through them.

**1. Find the mechanism.** Between the idea and the artifact is a leap: *which single concrete move embodies
this takeaway?* — the data's own shape, a figure cut from the void, one mark reframed three ways, two forces
colliding. Naming that mechanism is the craft; "content as form" is its target. No mechanism yet means you
still have a *topic*, not a composition. **For a recognizable subject, the most seductive false mechanism is
enlarging its own asset** — blowing up the existing logo or wordmark *feels* on-brand but only restates the
topic; reimagining means transforming the idea into a new move, not scaling the mark you were handed. (Worked:
an Amazon study that enlarged the a→z logo stayed inert; the one that made a single item *surface from the
whole catalogue* came alive.)

**2. Constrain the kit, then generate.** Fix the vocabulary before placing a mark — one ground family, **one
reserved accent**, a tight type set, a consistent chrome — and compose only within it. Coherence (and, across
a series, a family resemblance) falls out of the constraint, not from taste added later. *(Principle 5 used
as a production discipline.)*

**3. Make it, look at it cold, revise — the first version is never right.** Composition is **verified by the
eye, not predicted in the head.** Produce the artifact at final size, look at the *finished image* (not the
source you wrote), and read it as a stranger would — every worked plate took at least two passes. Run the
3-second test as a per-pass checklist; fix and remake until a cold look clears all of it:

- **Show, don't describe** — if the caption carries the idea and the picture doesn't, it failed.
- **The eye lands on the subject** — name where it goes first; a bright, filled, or central mark on the wrong
  element sinks the piece under a correct headline (salience; the absence-claim trap).
- **The demonstration is unambiguous** — does the emergent form read as intended (the swarm as *an arrow*,
  the disc as *sun*) *before* the caption, or only after? If it needs the words, rebuild the mechanism.
- **No collisions, no accidental void** — labels clear each other; no dead quadrant unless the emptiness *is*
  the point.
- **A "fresh" redo changed the *form*, not just its arrangement** — re-tidying the same device reads as the
  same piece.
- **Transformation, not enlargement** — for a known brand, is this its own logo/asset blown up (the topic
  restated), or a fresh move that *embodies* the idea?
- **For a series, review the pieces together** — side by side, repetition and cliché surface that a single
  view hides; recut them.

## Myths — do NOT use

- **[MYTH] Visual-weight / centre-of-mass balance.** The Arnheim–Ross "balance when the centre of mass
  sits on the axes" model failed five empirical tests. *Unproven*, not "balance is fake" — but don't
  compose by it.
- **[MYTH] The golden ratio.** φ attributions to the Parthenon, Mona Lisa, Vitruvian Man are baseless. Use
  a real grid; never justify a composition by φ.
- **[MYTH/marketing] "AI applies the rule of thirds for you."** Base text-to-image models do not reliably
  control layout, eye-path, or spatial relations — composition is still imposed by the human. (See the
  research file's AI-frontier section.)

## How the design-craft skills fit together

design-craft is **one toolkit of nine skills**, and **the orchestrator is the art-director** — it sets the
theme, feel and concept of a piece and coordinates the rest; **developing-style** invents a new style when the
brief needs one. This skill, composition-and-design-theory, is a
**specialist lever** the director deploys (arrangement, hierarchy, gestalt, figure-ground, meaning) — it does
*not* direct, set the feel, or own theme/mood.

**The director (the orchestrator)** names the objective and the *feel*, chooses the approach **by fit**
(restraint or boldness, convention or deviation, rich or reduced — never by reflex), protects imagery and
atmosphere, then deploys the specialists and judges each against the feel:
- arrangement / hierarchy / structure / meaning → **composition** *(this skill)*
- colour, contrast, palettes → **graphical-perception-and-color**
- typefaces, scale, measure → **typography**
- which chart form, data-ink → **charting**
- screen layout, navigation → **interface-ux-and-layout**
- identity, personality, mood, luxury cues → **branding**
- applying design-craft's own house look → **house-style-templates**

**All skills apply on every piece** — the director (the orchestrator) plus all the specialists (composition,
colour, type, charting, layout, branding, developing-style, house-style-templates) work together, every time; the director doesn't pick
one lever, it conducts the whole set. **brand-absorption is the one exception** — it activates only when
working inside an *existing* brand (facelift / reskin / client identity); see below.

**Working inside an existing brand** (facelift, reskin, client identity): get the binding **Brand Direction**
from **brand-absorption** first — it **overrides design-craft's own defaults and the house look**
(**house-style-templates** yields), and the director and every specialist serve *its* values. (The inverse of the
data-grid rule, where design-craft overrides a *library's* defaults.)

**The floors bind everyone** — WCAG contrast + never-colour-alone (colour), legibility and minimum sizes
(type), chart honesty / no truncated baselines (charting), and composition's medium-agnostic +
demonstrate-don't-reproduce guards. None yields for effect.

*In one line:* **the orchestrator directs the feel and coordinates; developing-style invents a new style when the
brief needs one · composition and the rest are levers it deploys · the floors bind all — and an absorbed Brand
Direction outranks design-craft's own defaults.**
