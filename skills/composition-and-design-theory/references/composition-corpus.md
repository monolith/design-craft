#### Source `a199839c` — quality: **primary** · published: 2011-10-19
- **[central]** Arnheim's Gestalt/physicalist theory of visual balance (derived from Denman Ross) — that a composition is balanced when its centre of mass aligns with the frame's major horizontal, vertical, or diagonal axes — was tested empirically across five studies and the detailed results did NOT support it. This reframes a widely cited composition 'principle' as unproven.
  > "the detailed results did not support the Arnheim–Ross theory"
- **[central]** When compositional elements were varied in physical 'weight' (disk size, greyness, or background), viewers did not re-frame to rebalance them: in the Two-Disks study participants placed frames identically regardless of the disks' properties, directly contradicting the weight-based balance model.
  > "When disk sizes, greyness, or backgrounds changed, participants consistently placed frames identically to when disks were uniform—contradicting the model's predictions."
- **[supporting]** Viewers can show an actual preference for OFF-axis compositions, not on-axis ones: the Spider-Web study found a significant preference for off-axis images, the opposite of what the centre-of-mass-on-axis theory predicts.
  > "Study 4 (Spider-Web): Significant preference for off-axis images, contradicting predictions."
- **[supporting]** There is a measurable but weak statistical tendency for acclaimed/real compositions to sit nearer the axes than random ones (art photographs 46.6% vs random 39.7%), but this is confounded and may reflect a preference for overall symmetry rather than centre-of-mass axis alignment specifically.
  > "Study 1 found art photographs had CoMs closer to axes (46.6%) versus random photographs (39.7%), statistically significant but potentially explainable by other factors."
- **[supporting]** Aesthetic preference in composition is governed by factors beyond physical/mechanical balance — the mechanical 'centre of mass' analogy to physics is insufficient to explain how viewers judge a composition.
  > "participants failed to adjust framing accordingly, suggesting factors beyond physical balance govern aesthetic preference in composition"

#### Source `a218d466` — quality: **primary** · published: 2012-07-30 (Online First; Psychological Bulletin, 2012). Note: this PDF is Part I, 'Perceptual Grouping and Figure-Ground Organization,' not Part II as the task title states; same authors and venue.
- **[central]** The Gestalt grouping principles form an enumerable, defined set that governs how separate visual elements bind into perceived units: the classical set is proximity, similarity, common fate, good continuation, closure, symmetry, and parallelism, extended by newer principles (synchrony, common region, element/uniform connectedness). These are exactly the levers a composition uses to make marks read as a grouped whole.
  > "we review the principles of grouping, both classical (e.g., proximity, similarity, common fate, good continuation, closure, symmetry, parallelism) and new (e.g., synchrony, common region, element and uniform connectedness), and their role in contour integration and completion"
- **[central]** A perceived whole (Gestalt) is not additive — it is qualitatively different from the sum of its parts and arises from global, not element-by-element, processes. This is the perceptual basis for treating a layout as a unified visual field rather than a collection of independent objects.
  > "Such structures or Gestalten are different from the sum of the parts. They arise from continuous global processes in the brain, rather than combinations of elementary excitations."
- **[central]** Gestalt structure is inherently figure-ground and hierarchical: a coherent form segregates from its background and organizes its parts around an inner center to which other parts relate hierarchically. This grounds negative/active space and visual hierarchy as perceptual facts, not stylistic preferences.
  > "They are structures that are segregated from the background, often with an inner center, to which the other parts are related hierarchically."
- **[supporting]** Individual grouping principles have concrete, falsifiable operational definitions — e.g., common fate: elements moving in the same way group together; closure: elements forming a closed figure group together — giving designers testable rules for what the eye will bind.
  > "elements that move in the same way tend to be grouped together"
- **[supporting]** Prägnanz (the law of good Gestalt / simplicity / minimum principle) was advanced as the single overarching law subsuming all the others, but the source explicitly flags that it was never rigorously defined — separating a real perceptual tendency toward simplicity from the unproven, vague 'master law' framing.
  > "Prägnanz was proposed as the fundamental law encompassing all the others but its formulation was left intentionally vague"

#### Source `a2c5b464` — quality: **secondary** · published: 2015-04-13
- **[central]** There is no scientific support for the golden ratio (~1.618) being the basis of aesthetic beauty in art, architecture, or design; the link is a myth/urban legend, not a proven compositional principle.
  > "The golden ratio's aesthetic bona fides are an urban legend, a myth, a design unicorn. Many designers don't use it, and if they do, they vastly discount its importance. There's also no science to really back it up."
- **[central]** In repeated rectangle-preference experiments, people do not pick the golden rectangle — they choose seemingly at random and inconsistently across trials, undercutting the claim that the golden ratio is intrinsically more pleasing (Keith Devlin, Stanford, ongoing unpublished study).
  > "If there were any truth behind the idea that the golden ratio is key to beautiful aesthetics, the students would pick out the rectangle closest to a golden rectangle. But they don't. They pick seemingly at random. And if you ask them to repeat the exercise, they pick different rectangles."
- **[supporting]** A Berkeley Haas School of Business study found consumers prefer rectangles in the aspect-ratio range 1.414 to 1.732; the golden rectangle (~1.618) falls inside that band but is not the clear favorite, so preference is a broad zone rather than a single 'golden' point.
  > "A study from the Haas School of Business at Berkeley found that, on average, consumers prefer rectangles that are in the range of 1.414 and 1.732. The range contains the golden rectangle, but its exact dimensions are not the clear favorite."
- **[central]** Working designers and architects do not use the golden ratio as a governing rule; at best it is an optional rule-of-thumb comparable to the rule of thirds, freely rejected, and treated as a tool rather than a dogmatic principle.
  > "at best, the golden ratio is as important to designers as any other compositional rule, such as the rule of thirds: maybe a fine rule-of-thumb, but one that good designers will feel free to reject."
- **[supporting]** The golden-ratio-as-beauty belief is a 19th-century invention: misattributed to Luca Pacioli in 1799 (per Mario Livio) and popularized by Adolf Zeising via arbitrarily chosen body measurements, i.e. patterns imposed rather than found.
  > "Zeising argued that the golden ratio could be applied to the human body by taking the height from a person's navel to his toes, then dividing it by the person's total height. These are just arbitrary body parts, crammed into a formula, Devlin says: "When measuring anything as complex as the human body, it's easy to come up with examples of ratios that are very near to 1.6.""

#### Source `a3487cde` — quality: **primary** · published: 1981 (original edition; this Internet Archive digitization uploaded 2017-08-24)
- **[central]** The grid's core function is to divide a 2D plane (or 3D space) into smaller fields/compartments so that all design elements — typography, photography, illustration, and colour — can be disposed within a single ordering system. This is the cross-cutting, medium-agnostic definition of the grid as composition tool.
  > "The grid divides a two-dimensional plane into smaller fields or a three-dimensional space into smaller compartments. [...] By means of this division into grid fields the elements of design, viz. typography, photography, illustration and colour, can be disposed in a better way."
- **[central]** Use of the grid is not merely practical but the expression of a constructive, objective mental attitude — the designer 'submitting to laws of universal validity' — embodying the will to systematize, clarify, and reach essentials. This grounds grid composition in a stated design philosophy rather than personal taste.
  > "The use of the grid as an ordering system is the expression of a certain mental attitude inasmuch as it shows that the designer conceives his work in terms that are constructive and oriented to the future. [...] Working with the grid system means submitting to laws of universal validity."
- **[supporting]** Images and data are modularized to the grid: every illustration, photo, or statistic occupies an integer number of grid fields (1, 2, 3 or 4), which produces uniformity across the presentation. This is a concrete, transferable compositional move (modular sizing) distinct from style.
  > "All illustrations, photographs, statistics etc. have the size of 1, 2, 3 or 4 grid fields, creating a certain uniformity in the presentation of visual information."
- **[supporting]** The grid binds typography and image to one shared measure: field height equals a specific number of text lines and field width equals column width — a proportional/modular structure linking text and picture to the same dimensional system.
  > "The height of the fields corresponds to a specific number of text lines; the width of the fields is identical with the width of the columns."
- **[supporting]** Reducing the number of visual elements and incorporating them into the grid produces compact planning, intelligibility, and clarity — i.e., restraint plus systematic placement is what yields legibility, an evidence-style design assertion (less, ordered, reads clearer).
  > "The reduction of the number of visual elements used and their incorporation in a grid system creates a sense of compact planning, intelligibility and clarity."

#### Source `a4bf43a2` — quality: **secondary** · published: 2016-08-30 (updated 2026-03-02)
- **[central]** Gestalt grouping principles are governed by an overarching law that the perceived whole is distinct from (not merely additive of) its component parts, which is why composition operates at the level of grouped wholes rather than isolated elements.
  > "The whole is other than the sum of the parts. — Kurt Koffka"
- **[central]** Proximity is a falsifiable grouping rule: elements placed close together are perceived as one unit and as separate from elements placed farther apart, regardless of their individual appearance.
  > "We group closer-together elements, separating them from those farther apart."
- **[central]** Similarity causes elements that share superficial visual characteristics (e.g., shape, color, size) to be perceived as a group even when spatially separated, making shared visual attributes a grouping mechanism independent of position.
  > "When items, objects or elements share superficial characteristics, we perceive them as grouped."
- **[supporting]** Closure (reification) means the visual system automatically completes incomplete shapes by filling gaps, so a composition can imply a form without rendering it fully.
  > "We prefer complete shapes, so we automatically fill the gaps between elements."
- **[supporting]** Prägnanz is the tendency to simplify complex visual information into the simplest possible basic shapes, providing the perceptual basis for reduction and minimalism in composition.
  > "Pragnanz describes the human tendency to simplify complexity."

#### Source `a70267fd` — quality: **primary** · published: 2025-10-22
- **[central]** In a controlled 3x6 within-subjects experiment (N=57) on two circular elements, layout STRUCTURE type — not size — is the dominant driver of perceived visual hierarchy, and overlapping (occluding) structures produce significantly stronger hierarchy than tangential (shared-edge) or separated structures. This is a transferable composition principle: overlap is the strongest single hierarchy cue, size is only supplementary.
  > "structural type emerges as the dominant element shaping hierarchical perception, with overlapping structures showing pronounced hierarchical suggestive characteristics that significantly outperform tangential and separated structures. Size can partially regulate this effect."
- **[supporting]** The mechanism behind overlap's hierarchy strength is figure-ground organization plus closure: the occluding element reads as foreground and the occluded element as background, with the brain auto-completing the hidden part. This grounds the composition move of deliberate overlap/occlusion in named Gestalt mechanisms (figure-ground + closure), medium-agnostically.
  > "The core of overlapping structures lies in the introduction of graphic occlusion, where occlusion provides “clues” such that the brain perceives the obscured object as background and the obscuring element as foreground [...] automatically filling in the missing parts of the graphic."
- **[supporting]** Proximity/distance alone does NOT establish hierarchy: when two elements are equal in size, tangential and separated arrangements show no significant difference in perceived hierarchy, and size disparity is what makes a separated arrangement read as hierarchical — outperforming a shared edge. This qualifies the conventional 'closer = grouped/ranked' reading of the proximity principle.
  > "When dimensions are equal, tangential and separated structures show no significant difference in perceived hierarchy. However, with the aid of size disparity, the hierarchical sense of separated structures can be markedly enhanced, yielding an effect stronger than that produced by shared edges."
- **[supporting]** Within a fixed structure type, varying the distance between equal-size elements does not change perceived hierarchy — a contrarian, falsifiable result that the proximity principle interacts with size/structure rather than acting independently. Distance modulated which structure formed (overlap vs tangent vs separate), but had no standalone effect on ranking once size was held equal.
  > "This indicates that, when icons are of equal size, distance does not influence hierarchical perception."
